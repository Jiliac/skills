# Harris Thriller Planner

Map historical events onto thriller structure using the Robert Harris method. This skill provides the pre-writing architecture for transforming sprawling historical timelines into tightly coiled political thrillers.

## When to Use

Use this skill when you need to:

- Select the right POV character for a historical thriller (the "Tiro" technique)
- Define your novel's ending before beginning the first draft
- Map messy historical chronology onto thriller beats
- Identify and compress historical "dead zones"
- Structure a historical novel as a procedural thriller, not a biography

## What This Skill Does NOT Do

This is pre-writing planning. It does NOT include:

- Drafting scenes (that's `historical-scene-drafting` skill)
- Research for sensory details (that's `historical-fiction-research` skill)
- Revision passes (that's `thriller-revision` and `historical-authenticity-review` skills)

## Core Elements

### 1. The Tiro POV Framework (Narrator Designer)

The most critical structural decision: select an observer narrator, not the Great Man.

**Why genius is best described from the outside:** If Cicero narrates his own rise to power, the text reads as arrogant or becomes a dry history book. An observer narrator maintains the Great Man's mystery and threat.

**Three strict criteria:**

1. **Unrestricted access** - Logical reason to be in the room (secretary, aide, ghostwriter, intelligence officer)
2. **Subordinate status** - Lacks power to change outcomes (powerlessness = thriller tension)
3. **Investigative role** - Professional reason to uncover/compile events (biography, intelligence, investigation)

**Examples:** Tiro (_Imperium_), Picquart (_An Officer and a Spy_), ghostwriter (_The Ghost_)

**Why this works:** Observer operates from information deficit, must deduce actively. Preserves Great Man's mystery. Creates blind spots, paranoia, unverified intelligence. Reader experiences events as terrified subordinate.

**Avoid:** Omniscient narrators (no surprise) or multiple POVs (textbook feel, destroys mystery).

### 2. The Ending-First Method (Ending Anchor)

Know the precise ending before writing the first paragraph. Treat the novel as a fixed journalistic assignment.

**The John Irving rule:** "Any writer who embarks on a novel without knowing how it is going to end is a fool and a knave. A novel recounts something that has already happened - therefore you cannot just make it up as you go along."

**Why it works:** A journalist reporting a plane crash already knows the outcome. The job is structuring how/why the inevitable occurred. Same retrospective certainty for novels.

**Application:** Define exact final scene before drafting. Establish immovable destination. Every scene accelerates toward endpoint. Plant foreshadowing, construct tight arcs.

**Prevents:** Structural meandering, historical bloat, "pantser" chaos, velocity loss in research.

**Mental shift:** Anxious creator → determined reporter assembling a defined puzzle.

### 3. Thriller Beat Mapping (Thriller Mapper)

History provides setting, characters, and outcome. Thriller structure provides pacing, scene selection, and tone.

**Step 1: Select for thematic resonance** - Choose events reflecting contemporary anxieties. Find arenas for high-stakes games, not eras to explore. (_Pompeii_ = American power; _Cicero_ = "West Wing on Tiber"; _Officer and Spy_ = whistleblower procedural)

**Step 2: Isolate procedural mechanism** - Find thriller trope in historical record: detective story, fugitive chase, locked-room mystery, espionage procedural.

**Step 3: Modernize lexicon** - Avoid "thee/thou" pastiche. Translate to modern equivalents. Roman politics → "canvass for votes," "running mate," "the ticket." Bridges temporal gap, no dense exposition needed.

**Step 4: Impose ticking clock** - History unfolds slowly; thrillers compress. _Munich_ (4 days), _Conclave_ (72 hours), _Fear Index_ (single day). Find historical deadline, build exclusively around countdown.

### 4. Dead Zone Compression

Historical timelines contain months/years where nothing happens dramatically. Ruthlessly identify and excise these.

**Identifying dead zones:** If no assassination/trial/disaster/crisis/betrayal → dramatically inert. Compress or skip.

**Compression techniques:**

1. Structural division (trilogy: rise/power/fall, not single chronicle)
2. Chapter breaks (leap over boring years)
3. Transitional paragraph ("Three months passed...")
4. In media res drops (brief time-note, then high-stakes room)

**Target:** Feels like "real-time" despite skipping months/years. Drama during crisis, not peace between.

**Harris's rule:** Choose tight time-frame, then shorten further. Cut anything that doesn't drive plot or escalate tension.

## Planning Workflow

### Phase 1: POV Selection

1. List historical figures; identify who has macro-power
2. Find subordinate with access, observational role, vulnerability
3. Test: Creates information deficit for reader? (Good = yes)

### Phase 2: Ending Definition

1. Identify historical endpoint; define emotional beat of final scene
2. Know last paragraph before writing first
3. Work backwards: What makes ending inevitable?

### Phase 3: Beat Mapping

1. Pull historical timeline; identify thriller trope
2. Map events onto beats: inciting incident → complications → midpoint reversal → darkest moment → climax → resolution
3. Translate historical terminology to modern equivalents

### Phase 4: Dead Zone Identification

1. Mark periods with no dramatic events
2. Decide: Skip (chapter break), compress (transitional paragraph), or imply offscreen
3. Target: Every scene = crisis point
4. Test: Can I cut this without losing plot? (If yes, cut)

## Quick Reference

**Tiro POV checklist:**

- Access to power? (Yes/No)
- Subordinate status? (Yes/No)
- Investigative role? (Yes/No)
- Information deficit for reader? (Yes/No)

**Ending-first test:**

- Can you write the final paragraph right now? (If no, don't start drafting)
- Does every scene accelerate toward this ending? (If no, cut the scene)

**Thriller beat questions:**

- What's the procedural mechanism? (Investigation, chase, trial, election)
- What's the ticking clock? (Hours, days, specific deadline)
- What modern genre does this historical event resemble?

**Dead zone audit:**

- Does this period contain assassination/trial/disaster/betrayal? (If no, compress)
- Could I skip this year without losing plot? (If yes, skip it)
