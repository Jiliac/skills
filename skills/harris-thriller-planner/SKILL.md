# Historical Thriller Planner

Map historical events onto thriller structure. This skill provides pre-writing architecture for transforming historical material into page-turners — whether political thrillers (Harris model) or business-building stories with a crisis turn (Garrett model).

## When to Use

Use this skill when you need to:

- Select the right POV character for a historical thriller
- Validate that your procedural engine can sustain a novel
- Define your novel's ending before beginning the first draft
- Choose between ticking-clock structure and business-building structure
- Map messy historical chronology onto thriller beats
- Identify and compress historical dead zones

## What This Skill Does NOT Do

This is pre-writing planning. It does NOT include:

- Drafting scenes (that's `historical-scene-drafting`)
- Research for sensory details (that's `historical-fiction-research`)
- Revision passes (that's `thriller-revision` and `historical-authenticity-review`)
- Series architecture (that's `builder-arc-planner`)

## Core Elements

### 1. Engine Validation (Do This First)

Before any structural decision, test whether your procedural engine can sustain a novel. The engine is the central mechanism that generates plot — a trial, an investigation, a political campaign, a business empire under threat, a natural disaster.

**The Five-Test Check:**

| Test                             | Question                                                              | Fail example                                  |
| -------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| **Physical stakes**              | Can people be arrested, exiled, killed, or ruined?                    | A tax audit where the worst outcome is a fine |
| **Adversarial contact**          | Does the protagonist confront other people in rooms?                  | A man reorganizing his own filing system      |
| **Public arenas**                | Do things happen where witnesses matter?                              | Everything occurs in a private study          |
| **Professional skill as weapon** | Does the protagonist's competence save or endanger them in real time? | The protagonist's skill is thinking quietly   |
| **Discovery**                    | Does the protagonist learn something they weren't supposed to see?    | The protagonist already knows everything      |

An engine must pass ALL FIVE tests. If it fails even one, either raise the stakes or change the engine.

**The Lean-Forward Test:** Describe your central conflict in one sentence to someone who knows nothing about the period. Do they lean forward or shrug?

- Shrug: "A merchant reorganizes his bookkeeping before an audit."
- Lean forward: "A woman secretly running a silk empire must hold it together when a political purge destroys her father."

If you get a shrug, the engine is wrong. Do not proceed to plotting.

**The Amplifier Rule:** The Harris method (ticking clock, thriller beats, dead zone compression) is an amplifier, not a generator. It organizes and sharpens existing drama. It cannot create drama from quiet material. Find the drama first. Apply the method second.

### 2. POV Selection

The most critical structural decision. There are two viable approaches — choose based on what your protagonist DOES.

**Option A: Observer Narrator (The Tiro Model)**

An observer narrator works when ALL FOUR conditions are met:

1. **The protagonist performs visibly in public arenas.** Oratory, trials, elections, public confrontations. There must be something dramatic to WATCH.
2. **There is a fame/knowledge gap.** The protagonist knows more than the narrator. The gap creates genuine mystery about the protagonist's plan.
3. **The narrator discovers the plan gradually.** The reader and narrator figure it out together. This generates suspense.
4. **The narrator has a separate, conflicting stake.** The narrator isn't just a camera. Their interests sometimes diverge from the protagonist's.

**Examples:** Tiro in _Imperium_ (Cicero performs in the Forum; Tiro doesn't know the full strategy; Tiro's freedom is at stake).

**Observer narrator FAILS when:**

- The protagonist's main activity is thinking or deciding privately
- The narrator sees the same information as the protagonist (no gap)
- The narrator and protagonist want the same thing (no conflict)
- The dramatic action is internal (choosing which papers to show, making calculations)

**Option B: Protagonist as POV (The Default)**

Harris uses protagonist-as-POV MORE OFTEN than observer. It is the safer default.

Use protagonist POV when:

- The protagonist's skill is interior (reading rooms, calculating, negotiating)
- The protagonist operates through indirect means (proxies, intermediaries, social maneuvers)
- The reader needs to feel the protagonist's fear and calculation directly
- There is no natural observer figure with a conflicting stake

**Examples:** Attilius in _Pompeii_, March in _Fatherland_, Legat in _Munich_, Lomeli in _Conclave_.

**The Honest Test:** If your protagonist's most dramatic moments happen inside their head — choosing, calculating, fearing — the reader must be IN that head. An observer watching someone think produces "a man at a desk, described by another man at a desk."

### 3. Act Structure Selection

Two models available. Choose based on where the drama lives.

**Model A: Harris Ticking Clock (Crisis from Page 1)**

The story opens inside or immediately before a crisis. A tight time frame compresses all action.

Structure:

- **Opening:** Crisis is already underway or arrives within pages
- **Escalation:** Each chapter raises the stakes
- **Midpoint reversal:** What the protagonist thought was the problem is actually something worse
- **Darkest moment:** The protagonist's plan fails or is exposed
- **Climax:** Confrontation under maximum pressure
- **Resolution:** Survival at a cost

Time frames: Hours (_Fear Index_), days (_Munich_, _Conclave_), weeks (_Pompeii_).

Best for: Stories where a single historical crisis IS the plot. Investigations, trials, sieges, diplomatic crises.

**Model B: Garrett Business-Building (Fun and Games → System Strikes)**

The story opens with the protagonist building something. The social/political conflict is present as background pressure in Acts 1-2, then detonates as the dominant engine in Act 3.

Structure:

- **Act 1 (25%):** First deals, small victories. The protagonist establishes their method (proxy system, business model, network). The reader enjoys watching competence under constraints. Each deal is a mini set piece.
- **Act 2 (40%):** Bigger deals, bigger risks. The empire grows. The protagonist extends further than their protection can safely cover. A secondary danger builds in the background (the father's political connections, a rival's resentment, regulatory attention).
- **Act 3 (35%):** The background danger detonates (political purge, investigation, betrayal). The social constraint that was manageable in Acts 1-2 becomes an existential crisis. The protagonist must use everything they built to survive — or lose it.

Best for: Stories about empire-building by protagonists who face systemic barriers (gender, race, class, outsider status). The barrier is present from page 1 but only becomes the main antagonist in Act 3.

**Key principle:** Acts 1-2 must be exciting AS PURE BUSINESS STORY. If the fun-and-games section is boring without the Act 3 crisis, the structure is broken. Each deal must have its own stakes, its own tension, its own risk of ruin.

**The Banker test:** Bernard Garrett's story is entertaining in Acts 1-2 (real estate deals, bank acquisitions, teaching the proxy how to perform) even before the racial persecution hits in Act 3. If your Acts 1-2 need the Act 3 crisis to be interesting, you have a pacing problem.

### 4. The Ending-First Method (Ending Anchor)

Know the precise ending before writing the first paragraph.

**The John Irving rule:** "Any writer who embarks on a novel without knowing how it is going to end is a fool and a knave."

**Application:**

1. Define the exact final scene before drafting
2. Know the last paragraph before writing the first
3. Every scene accelerates toward the endpoint
4. Work backwards: what makes the ending inevitable?

**Prevents:** Structural meandering, historical bloat, velocity loss in research.

**For Garrett structure specifically:** The ending should deliver on BOTH the business story and the social-conflict story. The protagonist either saves the empire at a cost, loses the empire but saves the people, or performs a final brilliant maneuver that redefines what "winning" means.

### 5. Protagonist Desire & Moral Argument Diagnostic

After defining the ending and before beat mapping, articulate what the story is about beneath the plot — and define the protagonist's internal arc. This is a diagnostic layer, not a plot generator. It tests and enriches existing architecture; it does not replace the Harris/Garrett structure.

**Step 1: State the protagonist's desire and need.**

Desire is external and conscious — the goal driving the plot. Need is internal and usually hidden — what the protagonist must learn or change to have a better life.

```
DESIRE: [What the protagonist wants in THIS story — the surface engine]
PSYCHOLOGICAL NEED: [What flaw is hurting the protagonist themselves]
MORAL NEED: [How the protagonist is hurting others through their flaw]
GHOST: [Backstory event that caused the weakness — optional but useful]
LIE: [The false belief the protagonist operates under — one sentence]
```

Desire provides the plot spine. Need provides the ending's depth. If you can't articulate the need, the ending may land as plot resolution without emotional weight.

**Step 2: State the moral argument.**

The moral argument is not a message or a lesson. It is the thesis the story proves through action — what happens when someone with this flaw pursues this desire under this pressure.

```
THEME LINE: [One sentence — "This story argues that ___"]
MORAL QUESTION: [The question every major character answers differently]
CONTROLLING IDEA: [Value + Cause, extracted from the defined ending]
```

The controlling idea (McKee) works backward from your ending: "What does my ending prove?" Example: "Ambition preserves power when a woman becomes willing to sacrifice what she once loved most."

**Step 3: Run the beat diagnostic.**

After beat mapping (Phase 5), test each act against the moral argument. This is a validation pass — it identifies gaps without mandating specific plot changes.

```
BEAT DIAGNOSTIC (run per act):
Act 1: Which beat carries moral weight? [ ]
  - Does the protagonist's moral weakness appear in action? Y/N
  - Is the protagonist hurting someone through this weakness? Y/N
Act 2: Which beat carries moral weight? [ ]
  - Do the protagonist's morally costly actions intensify? Y/N
  - Does an ally challenge the protagonist's methods? Y/N
Act 3: Which beat carries moral weight? [ ]
  - Does the climax resolve which set of values is superior? Y/N
  - Does the ending contain a self-revelation OR a visible failure to achieve one? Y/N

FLAGS: [Acts with no moral beat — consider adding thematic layer to existing beats]
```

**The discipline:** Truby's moral argument operates on the character-theme layer; Harris's architecture operates on the plot-mechanics layer. They address different structural questions. The moral argument can add content to existing beats but cannot move or replace beats that serve Harris's procedural logic. If a moral gap coincides with a structurally necessary beat, the thematic layer wraps around the existing beat rather than displacing it.

**Arc type decision:** Does the protagonist achieve self-revelation (positive arc), achieve it too late (tragic arc), or fail to achieve it entirely (negative arc — _Godfather_ model, where the audience sees what the hero cannot)? State this before drafting. It determines whether the ending reads as transformation, tragedy, or moral cost.

### 6. Thriller Beat Mapping

**Step 1: Select for thematic resonance.** Choose events reflecting contemporary anxieties. Find arenas for high-stakes games, not eras to explore.

**Step 2: Isolate the procedural mechanism.** Find the thriller trope in the historical record: investigation, chase, trial, election, business deal, diplomatic crisis.

**Step 3: Modernize the lexicon.** Avoid pastiche. Translate to modern equivalents. Roman politics → "canvass for votes." Song fiscal policy → "the government just entered your market as a competitor."

**Step 4: Impose a ticking clock.** History unfolds slowly; thrillers compress. Find the historical deadline. For Garrett structure, each deal in Acts 1-2 has its own mini-clock; Act 3 has the main clock.

**Step 5: Run the moral argument beat diagnostic** (from Section 5, Step 3) against the completed beat map. Flag acts with no moral weight. Add thematic layers to existing beats where needed — do not restructure beats.

### 7. Dead Zone Compression

Historical timelines contain months/years where nothing happens dramatically.

**Identifying dead zones:** If no arrest/trial/disaster/crisis/betrayal/deal occurs → dramatically inert. Compress or skip.

**Compression techniques:**

1. Chapter breaks (leap over boring years)
2. Transitional sentences ("Three months passed...")
3. In media res drops into the next crisis
4. For Garrett structure: montage-style deal sequences for Acts 1-2 where individual deals are too small for full scenes

**Target:** Feels like real-time despite skipping months/years. Drama during crisis, not peace between.

## Planning Workflow

### Phase 1: Engine Validation

1. State your central conflict in one sentence
2. Apply the five-test check
3. Apply the lean-forward test
4. If the engine fails, find a more dramatic historical event — or raise the stakes

### Phase 2: POV Selection

1. Ask: does the protagonist perform publicly, or act through interior calculation and indirect means?
2. If public performance → consider observer narrator (test all four Tiro conditions)
3. If interior/indirect → use protagonist as POV (the default)

### Phase 3: Act Structure Selection

1. Does the story begin with a crisis already in progress? → Harris ticking clock
2. Does the story begin with building, then a crisis detonates later? → Garrett business-building
3. Hybrid: Garrett Acts 1-2, then Harris takes over for Act 3 (the crisis section)

### Phase 4: Ending Definition

1. Define the exact final scene
2. Know the last emotional beat before writing the first
3. Work backwards: what makes this ending inevitable?

### Phase 5: Desire, Need & Moral Argument

1. State the protagonist's desire (external goal) and need (psychological + moral)
2. State the ghost, lie/weakness, and arc type (positive / tragic / negative)
3. State the theme line, moral question, and controlling idea
4. These inform beat mapping but do not generate beats — they test them

### Phase 6: Beat Mapping

1. Pull historical timeline; identify thriller trope
2. Map events onto chosen act structure
3. For Garrett: identify 4-6 deal set pieces for Acts 1-2
4. For Harris: map inciting incident → complications → midpoint → darkest moment → climax
5. Translate historical terminology to modern equivalents
6. Run the moral argument beat diagnostic — flag acts with no moral weight

### Phase 7: Dead Zone Identification

1. Mark periods with no dramatic events
2. Decide: skip, compress, or montage
3. Test: can I cut this without losing plot? (If yes, cut)

## Quick Reference

**Engine validation:**

- Physical stakes? Adversarial contact? Public arenas? Skill as weapon? Discovery?
- One-sentence lean-forward test: do they lean forward or shrug?

**POV decision tree:**

- Protagonist performs publicly + observer has conflicting stake → Observer narrator
- Protagonist acts through calculation/indirection + no natural observer → Protagonist POV
- When in doubt → Protagonist POV (safer default)

**Act structure decision:**

- Story IS a crisis → Harris ticking clock
- Story is building → crisis → Garrett business-building
- Not sure → Which historical event excites you most? If it's a single crisis, Harris. If it's a rise-and-fall, Garrett.

**Ending-first test:**

- Can you write the final paragraph right now? (If no, don't start drafting)
- Does every scene accelerate toward this ending? (If no, cut the scene)

**Moral argument diagnostic:**

- Theme line stated? Moral question stated? Controlling idea extracted from ending?
- Each act has at least one beat carrying moral weight?
- Protagonist's desire and need are distinct? Arc type decided?
- The diagnostic tests existing beats — it does not generate or restructure them
