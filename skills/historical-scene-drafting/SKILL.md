# Historical Scene Drafting

Draft dialogue-heavy political scenes with period texture using the Robert Harris method. This skill focuses exclusively on getting scenes onto the page with natural dialogue, sensory grounding, and political dynamics.

## When to Use

Use this skill when you need to:

- Draft political negotiation scenes ("men in rooms")
- Write period-appropriate dialogue that sounds natural
- Ground scenes in sensory texture without slowing thriller pace
- Set up power dynamics through conversation
- Create tension in dialogue-heavy scenes without physical action

## What This Skill Does NOT Do

This is pure drafting. It does NOT include:

- Modern analog filter application (that's revision work - see `thriller-revision` skill)
- Exposition extraction (that's revision work - see `thriller-revision` skill)
- Anachronism auditing (that's research work - see `historical-fiction-research` skill)
- Pacing surgery or structural editing (that's revision work)

## Core Principles

### 1. Modern Syntax, Period Vocabulary

Write dialogue that uses natural speech rhythms while maintaining period-appropriate vocabulary.

**Good:** "The Senate will not stand for it."
**Bad (Museum Prose):** "Prithee, the Senate shall not brook such perfidy."
**Bad (Anachronistic):** "The Senate will totally freak out."

The key: Natural sentence structure + era-appropriate nouns/verbs + no modern slang.

### 2. Sensory Grounding Through Action Beats

Embed period texture directly into dialogue using action beats, not separate paragraphs.

**Weak:** "The trade negotiations have completely failed," Cromwell said tiredly.
**Strong:** Cromwell leaned close to the hearth, the pine smoke stinging his eyes. "The trade negotiations have completely failed."

Use action beats to:

- Identify speakers without tags
- Root characters in the physical environment
- Convey emotional states through body language
- Inject period texture without stopping dialogue flow

### 3. "Men in Rooms" Scene Architecture

Political scenes are dialogue combat. Structure them like fight scenes:

1. **Establish spatial dynamics** - Who sits where? Physical positioning = power hierarchy
2. **Inciting statement** - Open with a provocative demand or revelation
3. **Weaponized silence** - Powerful characters withhold responses strategically
4. **The pivot** - A sudden barb or unexpected leverage shifts the conversation
5. **Power reversal** - By scene end, the hierarchy must fundamentally shift

Every line of dialogue is an attack, parry, feint, or tactical retreat.

### 4. Power Dynamics in Conversation

Show power through dialogue mechanics:

- **Who interrupts?** Power players cut others off mid-sentence
- **Who speaks into silence?** Subordinates fill uncomfortable pauses
- **Who controls pacing?** Leaders decide when conversation continues
- **Who gets formal address?** "Your Majesty" vs. first names signals hierarchy
- **Who sits vs. stands?** Physical positioning reinforces status

### 5. Subtext Over Text

Characters bound by etiquette, law, or danger rarely say what they mean directly.

**Text (surface):** A polite debate about grain shipments to a province
**Subtext (actual stakes):** Starving a rival's voting base, funding a private army

The reader must understand the lethal subtext beneath courteous phrasing. Use viewpoint character's internal thoughts to decode the threat.

## Drafting Workflow

### Pre-Scene Planning

Define before writing:

- Spatial setup, power dynamics, stakes, subtext
- 2-3 sensory anchors (smoke, dust, temperature)

### Scene Structure

1. **Opening beat** - Establish spatial positioning + inciting statement
2. **Dialogue combat** - Short exchanges, embed exposition in conflict, deploy strategic silences
3. **Sensory grounding** - One action beat per 3-4 exchanges, micro-details only
4. **Closing beat** - Power shift or escalation

**Target ratio:** 70-80% dialogue, 20-30% action beats and internal reactions.

## Quick Reference

**Dialogue rules:** Avoid "thee/thou" pastiche, no modern slang/concepts, use era-appropriate profanity, class-appropriate metaphors. See `references/dialogue-principles.md`.

**Common failures:**

- Museum prose - Read aloud test; if you stumble, simplify
- Floating dialogue - Add action beats (1 per 3-4 exchanges minimum)
- Info-dumping - Characters argue over facts, not recite them
- Power-free dialogue - Show hierarchy through interruptions, silences, formal address
