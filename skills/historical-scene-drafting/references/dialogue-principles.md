# Dialogue Principles: Period Speech Without Museum Prose

## The Central Problem

Historical fiction dialogue faces two opposing failure modes:

1. **Museum prose** - Overly formal, archaic diction that sounds like translated primary sources
2. **Anachronism** - Modern slang or concepts that shatter temporal immersion

The goal: Navigate between these extremes to achieve natural speech with period texture.

## Core Philosophy

> "The past was once the present. Characters must be permitted to run about, ignorant of their fates, with all their mistakes unmade." - Hilary Mantel

Your characters are not historical exhibits. They are living people experiencing their era as an uncertain, chaotic present. They don't speak like museum plaques.

## The Three-Part Test

Every line of historical dialogue must pass three tests:

1. **Natural rhythm test** - Read it aloud. Do you stumble? Does it sound theatrical? If yes, simplify.
2. **Anachronism test** - Could this character think this thought with their era's worldview? (Not just vocabulary - conceptual framework)
3. **Character knowledge test** - Would they really explain this to each other, or are you info-dumping to the reader?

## Principle 1: Modern Syntax, Period Vocabulary

Use contemporary sentence structures with era-appropriate words.

### Good Examples

```
"The Senate will not stand for it."
"He holds the purse strings. If he closes them, we starve."
"You have doomed us all, Thomas."
"The King's mood is dark today. Tread lightly."
```

**Why these work:**

- Natural speech rhythm (subject-verb-object, active voice)
- No archaic grammatical constructions
- Period-appropriate institutional vocabulary (Senate, King, purse strings)
- Direct emotional stakes

### Bad Examples (Museum Prose)

```
"Prithee, the Senate shall not brook such perfidy."
"He is the master of our coin and treasury, as it hath always been."
"As you know, Henry VIII has recently executed his wife."
```

**Why these fail:**

- "Prithee," "hath," "brook" - archaic forms slow reading, sound theatrical
- Passive constructions ("is the master of") feel translated
- "As you know" exposition dump (characters explaining what they both know)

### Bad Examples (Anachronistic)

```
"Wow, Tom, you really messed this up for everyone."
"The Senate will totally freak out."
"This gives me major anxiety."
```

**Why these fail:**

- "Wow," "totally," "major" - modern intensifiers
- "Messed up," "freak out" - contemporary slang
- "Anxiety" - modern clinical/therapeutic framing of emotion

## Principle 2: Pastiche is Not Creative

> "Pastiche is not creative. It is a mechanical reproduction of old syntax. We must use words as arrows that pierce directly to the audience's core comprehension." - Hilary Mantel

Do NOT replicate the exact phrasing of primary sources. Historical documents were often:

- Heavily formal (legal, diplomatic, religious contexts)
- Translated poorly from other languages
- Curated for preservation, not natural speech
- Missing the casual, everyday register entirely

**The mistake:** Treating formal Tudor court records as evidence of how people actually spoke in private conversations.

**The reality:** Even kings spoke casually to intimates. We just don't have transcripts of those conversations.

## Principle 3: Avoid "Thee/Thou" Unless Essential

Early Modern English pronouns ("thee," "thou," "thy") are linguistic quicksand.

### When to avoid (95% of the time)

Don't use them just to "sound old." They slow reading velocity and create theatrical distance.

**Weak:**

```
"Thou hast betrayed me, and I shall have thy head for it."
```

**Strong:**

```
"You betrayed me. I'll have your head for this."
```

### When to use (5% of exceptions)

Only when the pronoun itself carries plot-critical meaning:

- **Deliberate insult** - Switching from formal "you" to intimate "thou" to show disrespect
- **Religious context** - Prayer to God ("Thee" in liturgy)
- **Legal formula** - Court charges read in archaic formal language

In these cases, the pronoun is the point of the line, not decoration.

## Principle 4: Class-Appropriate Metaphors

Characters speak in metaphors drawn from their lived experience.

### Peasant/Commoner Speech

Metaphors from:

- Farming (plowing, harvest, fallow fields, crop failure)
- Animal husbandry (breeding, breaking horses, culling herds)
- Weather and seasons
- Physical labor

**Example:**

```
"You're trying to plow a field of stones. It won't yield."
```

### Noble/Aristocratic Speech

Metaphors from:

- Hunting (falconry, coursing, bringing prey to ground)
- Warfare (siege, flanking, retreat)
- Lineage and blood (breeding, stock, inheritance)
- Land management (estates, tenants, rents)

**Example:**

```
"He's brought the King to ground like a wounded stag. Now he'll move in for the kill."
```

### Scholar/Cleric Speech

Metaphors from:

- Classical texts (Cicero, Aristotle, Church fathers)
- Theology (sin, grace, damnation, providence)
- Logic and disputation
- Books and manuscripts

**Example:**

```
"Your argument has the structure of a syllogism, but the premise is rotten."
```

## Principle 5: Era-Appropriate Profanity

Insults and curses root characters in their material reality and fears.

### Medieval/Early Modern Insults

Focus on:

- Disease ("pox-ridden," "leper," "plague-bearer")
- Animals ("cur," "whoreson dog," "spawn of vipers")
- Legitimacy ("bastard," "whoreson," "hedge-born")
- Damnation ("God's blood," "Hell take you," "Devil's spawn")

**Good:**

```
"You son of a pox-ridden cur."
"God's blood, you'll hang for this."
```

### Avoid Modern Clinical Language

**Bad:**

```
"You absolute sociopath."
"That's so toxic."
"You're gaslighting me."
```

Modern psychological/therapeutic vocabulary did not exist. Characters can observe manipulative behavior, but they frame it in moral (sin, wickedness, treachery) or social (dishonor, betrayal) terms, not clinical diagnosis.

## Principle 6: No Modern Idioms or Metaphors

Many common English idioms derive from technologies or contexts that didn't exist in historical periods.

### Dangerous Modern Idioms

| Idiom                 | Origin                    | Why It Fails                                        |
| --------------------- | ------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| "Blow off steam"      | Steam engines             | Pre-industrial eras had no steam power              |
| "Move the goalposts"  | Modern sports             | Organized sports with standardized rules are modern |
| "On the radar"        | Military radar technology | Pre-20th century                                    |
| "Jump the shark"      | 1970s TV show             | Absurdly modern                                     |
| "Touch base"          | Baseball                  | American sport, modern                              |
| "Throw under the bus" | Motorized vehicles        | Pre-automotive era                                  |

### Safe Timeless Idioms

These work across eras because they reference universal human experiences:

- "Crossing a bridge when we come to it"
- "Burning bridges"
- "A bird in the hand"
- "Throwing stones in glass houses"
- "Turning a blind eye"

## Principle 7: Emotional Directness Without Modern Slang

Characters should express strong emotion directly, but using period-appropriate intensifiers.

### Modern Intensifiers to Avoid

- "Totally," "literally," "absolutely," "completely" (modern overuse pattern)
- "So" as intensifier ("so angry," "so tired" - colloquial modern)
- "Like" as filler word
- "Actually," "basically," "honestly" (modern verbal tics)

### Period-Neutral Emotional Directness

**Weak (modern):**

```
"I'm literally so exhausted I could die."
```

**Strong (period-neutral):**

```
"I'm spent. I can't go on."
```

**Weak (modern):**

```
"This is absolutely insane."
```

**Strong (period-neutral):**

```
"This is madness."
```

## Principle 8: Contractions (Use Sparingly, Strategically)

Contractions existed in historical speech, but their overuse creates modern informality.

### When to use contractions

- **High emotion** - Characters drop formality under stress
- **Intimate relationships** - Family, close friends, lovers
- **Lower class speech** - Less formal register

**Example:**

```
"I can't do this anymore. I won't."
```

### When to avoid contractions

- **Formal settings** - Court, diplomatic negotiations, legal proceedings
- **Superior addressing subordinate** - Formality maintains power distance
- **Deliberate emphasis** - "I will not yield" (more forceful than "I won't")

## Principle 9: Sentence Length Variation

Modern readers expect varied rhythm. Historical prose (especially translated or formal) tends toward uniform, complex sentences.

### Bad (Uniform Complexity)

```
The ambassador, having arrived at the court in the early hours of the morning, presented his credentials to the chamberlain, who, after examining them with due scrutiny, conducted him to the presence chamber where the King awaited.
```

### Good (Varied Rhythm)

```
The ambassador arrived at dawn. He presented his credentials to the chamberlain, who examined them carefully, then led him to the King's presence chamber.
```

**Pattern to follow:**

- Short sentence (punchy impact)
- Medium sentence (narrative flow)
- Another short sentence (emphasis)
- Longer sentence (complexity or buildup)

## Principle 10: Exposition Discipline

Never have characters explain things they both already know just to educate the reader.

### Bad (Exposition Dump)

```
"As you know, Marcus, the Senate voted last week to grant emergency powers to the consul, giving him authority to raise additional legions without prior approval from the tribunes."
```

**Why it fails:** "As you know" signals the character wouldn't actually say this. It's narrator intrusion.

### Good (Embedded in Conflict)

```
"The consul's already raising three new legions."

Marcus grabbed his arm. "The Senate didn't authorize that."

"They gave him emergency powers last week. He doesn't need their permission anymore."
```

**Why it works:** Information emerges from character conflict. Marcus's surprise is natural. The exposition is weaponized - it's bad news, not a lecture.

## Advanced Technique: Dialogue as Character Differentiation

Different characters should have distinct speech patterns based on:

### Education Level

**Educated character:**

```
"The correlation between grain prices and civil unrest is well documented in Thucydides."
```

**Uneducated character:**

```
"When bread costs too much, people riot. Always have."
```

### Regional Origin

Use vocabulary choices and reference points, not phonetic spelling of accents.

**Capital city native:**

```
"The court's already heard about this. By noon, the King will know."
```

**Provincial character:**

```
"Word travels slow out past the city walls. We'll have time."
```

### Personality

**Cautious/Diplomatic:**

```
"We might consider... exploring alternative arrangements."
```

**Direct/Blunt:**

```
"That plan is suicide. Find another."
```

## The Read-Aloud Test

Final check for all dialogue:

1. Read it out loud
2. At normal speaking pace
3. If you stumble, simplify
4. If it sounds like a Renaissance Faire, cut the archaic words
5. If it sounds like a modern TV show, cut the slang

**Target:** Should sound like articulate humans having an urgent conversation, not a historical reenactment or a modern screenplay.

## Confidence in Breaking Rules

These principles are guidelines, not laws. You can break them when:

- The historical figure's actual documented words are too perfect to change
- A specific archaic term has no good modern equivalent and is essential
- The violation is deliberate for characterization (pretentious character using archaic forms)

But break them consciously, for a reason, not by default.
