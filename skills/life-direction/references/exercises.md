## Means Inventory (Effectuation)

Start from what you have, not what you wish you had. Sarasvathy's research on expert entrepreneurs: they derive goals from available means rather than acquiring means for predetermined goals.

**Three categories:**

| Category       | Prompt                                                                  | Examples                                                     |
| -------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Who I am       | Identity, genuine values (from Phase 1), personality, what energizes me | "I'm a systems builder who craves intellectual challenge"     |
| What I know    | Skills, domain expertise, reputation, unique knowledge combinations     | "AI systems, security research, 1100+ citations, French/English/Chinese" |
| Whom I know    | People, communities, weak ties, potential collaborators                 | "Co-founder François, KAIST network, AI safety community"    |

**For each item ask:** Am I using this, ignoring this, or actively avoiding this? Ignored and avoided assets are the richest direction-generators.

## Odyssey Plans (Designing Your Life)

Three distinct 3-year life directions, each with a concrete description and dashboard.

**Plan A:** Natural extension of current trajectory, done more intentionally.
**Plan B:** What you'd do if Plan A were suddenly impossible.
**Plan C:** What you'd do if money and reputation were irrelevant.

**Dashboard for each (rate 1-5):**

| Dimension  | Question                                            |
| ---------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Resources  | Do I have (or can I get) what this plan requires?   |
| Likability | Do I genuinely want this life?                      |
| Confidence | Can I actually pull this off?                       |
| Coherence  | Does this fit who I actually am (Phase 1 insights)? |

**Concreteness test:** Describe a typical Tuesday at 10am in this plan. Where are you? What are you doing? With whom? What does it feel like in your body?

**Testable assumptions:** For each plan, list 2-3 things that must be true for it to work. These become the targets for prototype conversations and experiments.

## Compensatory Goal Filter (3-Layer)

Apply sequentially to any candidate direction. Each layer catches what the previous misses.

### Layer 1: Goal Contents Filter (GCT)

Rate the direction's primary content:

- **Intrinsic content:** Personal growth, mastery, meaningful relationships, contribution to community
- **Extrinsic content:** Wealth as status marker, fame/recognition, image/appearance of success

If primarily extrinsic: "What's the intrinsic need underneath? Can the direction be reframed to serve that need directly?"

### Layer 2: Self-Concordance Filter (SCM)

Rate motivation on four subscales (1-5 each):

- External: "Others expect this of me"
- Introjected: "I'd feel guilty/ashamed if I didn't"
- Identified: "I genuinely believe this matters"
- Intrinsic: "I find this inherently interesting"

**Self-concordance index** = (identified + intrinsic) − (external + introjected)

- Score > 4: Strong alignment. Proceed.
- Score 0-4: Mixed. Investigate which subscale is inflated.
- Score < 0: Misaligned. This direction is driven by "shoulds," not values.

### Layer 3: Schema Activation Test

Imagine full success in this direction. Close eyes, picture it vividly.

- **What emotion arises?** Relief vs. vitality. Relief = compensatory ("I'm finally safe/enough"). Vitality = genuine ("This matters regardless").
- **Whose face appears?** If a specific person (father, critic, rival) → compensatory. If abstract or no one → more likely genuine.
- **Would failure feel like disappointment or annihilation?** Disappointment = healthy investment. Annihilation = identity-fused, likely compensatory.

## Self-Concordance Quick Assessment

For any single goal or direction, rate each statement 1 (not at all) to 5 (completely):

1. "I pursue this because somebody else wants me to, or because the situation demands it" (External)
2. "I pursue this because I would feel ashamed, guilty, or anxious if I didn't" (Introjected)
3. "I pursue this because I really believe it's an important goal to have" (Identified)
4. "I pursue this because of the fun and enjoyment it provides" (Intrinsic)

Index = (Q3 + Q4) − (Q1 + Q2). Range: -8 to +8.

## Money Chain Analysis

"If you had [target amount], what would you do?" → "And then what?" → repeat until reaching a terminal emotion.

Common terminal emotions: safe, worthy, free, loved, enough, respected, at peace.

**The terminal emotion is the actual goal.** The money is an instrumental strategy. Once identified, ask: "Is the direction you're considering the most direct path to this emotion? Or is there a shorter path?"

## Future Self Letter

Write a letter to yourself 20 years from now describing:

- The direction you chose and why
- What the first year looked like
- What you're proud of
- What you'd tell your current self

**Why 20 years, not 1 year:** Hershfield's research shows that farther temporal horizons produce stronger motivational effects for directional framing. The letter activates future-self continuity, which improves long-term decision quality.

**Key instruction:** Write from the body, not the résumé. Describe how the life *feels*, not what it *achieved*.

## Minimum Viable Experiment

A 2-week, low-cost, reversible test of a direction's core assumption.

**Design template:**

| Field                  | Entry                                              |
| ---------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Direction being tested | [Which Odyssey Plan or narrowed direction]          |
| Core assumption        | [What must be true for this to work]               |
| Experiment             | [Specific action over 2 weeks]                     |
| What I'll learn        | [What the result tells me about the assumption]    |
| Affordable loss        | [Maximum I'm willing to invest — time, money, ego] |
| Go/no-go signal        | [What result would confirm vs. disconfirm]         |

**Examples:**
- Direction: AI consulting → Experiment: Conduct 5 discovery calls with potential clients this week
- Direction: Writing/teaching → Experiment: Publish 3 posts and measure engagement + felt experience
- Direction: Product company → Experiment: Build a landing page and run $100 in ads to test demand

## Direction Statement Formula

One sentence. A 12-year-old could understand it. No jargon, no qualifications.

**Structure:** "For the next 12 months, I am [doing what] [for whom] [because it serves my value of X]."

**Examples:**
- "For the next 12 months, I am building an AI consulting practice serving mid-market companies because it fully engages my technical ability and lets me solve real problems."
- "For the next 12 months, I am writing and teaching about AI systems because I want to be known for my thinking and build an intellectual community."

**Test:** Read it aloud. Does your body lean forward or pull back? Forward = proceed. Back = investigate.

## Affordable Loss Assessment

Effectuation principle: decide based on what you can afford to lose, not what you might gain.

**For the 12-month committed experiment, define:**

| Dimension        | Maximum affordable loss                                   |
| ---------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| Time             | [How many months can I invest before needing income?]     |
| Money            | [How much savings/runway can I allocate?]                 |
| Reputation       | [What professional risk am I taking? Is it recoverable?]  |
| Opportunity cost | [What am I saying no to? Can I return to it later?]       |
| Relationships    | [Does this direction strain any key relationships?]       |

If the affordable loss for any dimension exceeds what the direction requires, proceed. If not, either reduce the experiment's scope or choose a less costly direction.
