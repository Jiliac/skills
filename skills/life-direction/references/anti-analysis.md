# Anti-Analysis Protocol for Direction-Setting

Direction-setting is the **most dangerous phase for an analytical mind**. Self-exploration has a natural emotional floor (you hit feelings). Performance coaching has external accountability (you either did the thing or didn't). Direction-setting has neither — it's a wide-open field where analysis can run forever without ever being tested against reality.

## The Core Risk

The user substitutes "choosing the optimal direction" for "choosing a direction and testing it." More analysis feels like progress but produces no data that prototyping wouldn't provide faster.

## Three Detection Markers

### 1. Option Generation Without Narrowing

The user keeps adding new possible directions instead of filtering existing ones. "What about X?" when they already have three viable plans is almost always avoidance of choosing.

**Response:** "You have three strong options. Adding a fourth won't make the choice clearer — it'll make it harder. Which of your three plans are you ready to eliminate?"

### 2. Research Mode Instead of Experiment Mode

The user wants to read more, analyze more market data, study more examples before acting. They frame it as due diligence.

**Response:** "You're in research mode. Research feels productive but generates no data that a 2-week experiment wouldn't generate faster. What's the smallest test you could run this week?"

### 3. Criteria Refinement Instead of Application

The user keeps refining the filter framework rather than applying it to their actual choices. Perfecting the rubric to avoid using it.

**Response:** "The filter is good enough. Apply it now to your three plans. What scores do you get? If the scores are close, that's a signal that analysis has hit its ceiling — the difference will only show up in testing."

## The Interrupt Sequence

**First instance:** "That's a useful insight. Which of your plans does it change? If none, let's move on."

**Second consecutive:** "We're designing, not researching. You have enough information to choose at 70% certainty. What's stopping you?"

**Third consecutive:** Name it directly: "We're in analysis mode. More thinking won't improve this decision — the research is clear on that. You need to test, not think. Which direction will you prototype this week?"

## Phase-Specific Traps

### "I need to understand myself better first"

The user retreats to Phase 1 (self-exploration) when direction-setting gets uncomfortable. They have enough self-knowledge — the discomfort is about commitment, not understanding.

**Response:** "You understand yourself well enough. The question isn't who you are — you answered that in self-exploration. The question is what you'll build. That's answered by doing, not by more inner work."

### "What if I choose wrong?"

Analytical minds believe there's a "right" answer findable through sufficient analysis. There isn't under deep uncertainty.

**Response:** "No amount of analysis can tell you the right direction in advance. Dan Gilbert's research shows firm commitment triggers satisfaction that tentative exploration doesn't. Choosing 'wrong' for 12 months generates more useful data than deliberating for 12 months."

### "I need more data"

The user wants another prototype conversation, another market analysis, another mentor's opinion.

**Response:** "You have enough data to choose. You'll never have enough data to be certain — that's the nature of life direction. The additional data you need comes from committing and executing, not from more research."

## The 70% Rule

Enforce explicitly: "If you're 70% sure, that's enough. Choose. The remaining 30% can only be resolved through action. Waiting for 90% certainty costs more than the extra 20% is worth."

## Structural Safeguards Built Into the Sessions

- Every session produces a behavioral output (not an insight)
- Prototype conversations and experiments force contact with reality
- Session 4 requires narrowing to one direction — the program doesn't let you stay in three forever
- Session 5's commitment framing explicitly activates the psychological immune system
- Session 8's commitment ceremony is somatic, not analytical — the body says yes, not the spreadsheet
