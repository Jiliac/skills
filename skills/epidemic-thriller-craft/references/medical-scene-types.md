# Medical Scene Types

Nine scene types for epidemic fiction, drawn from Cook, Gerritsen, Preston, Brooks, and Gabaldon, synthesized with physician-novelist craft guidance.

## Full Scene Type Taxonomy

| Scene Type                | What Happens                                                    | Tension Source                                                                         | Sensory Register                                                                   |
| ------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Diagnosis**             | Protagonist reads symptoms as clues; forms hypothesis; tests it | Wrong initial hypothesis; symptoms that don't fit; the recognition moment              | Close body observation — skin color, breathing, pulse, smell                       |
| **Triage**                | Sorting patients by severity under resource constraint          | Forced moral choice: who gets treatment? Who is left to die?                           | Crowded space, competing cries, hands moving body to body                          |
| **Intervention**          | Treatment attempt under pressure                                | Complication mid-procedure; uncertain outcome; ticking clock of deterioration          | Hands on the body — sweat, blood, resistance or yielding of tissue                 |
| **Teaching**              | Protagonist explains medical concepts to non-experts            | Stakes-laden exposition; resistance from students trained in different paradigm        | Bedside demonstration, gesture, the student's face                                 |
| **Failure**               | Patient dies despite protagonist's best effort                  | Shift from frantic action to stillness; emotional aftermath                            | Body changes — breathing stops, color drains, the living recoil                    |
| **Public Health**         | Implementing population-level measures                          | Individual freedom vs. collective survival; community resistance; official obstruction | Scale shifts outward — streets, neighborhoods, walls, water systems                |
| **Autopsy / Examination** | Reading the dead body for information                           | Discovery of something unexpected; the dead body as text                               | Clinical precision — cold flesh, interior revealed, anomaly rewrites the narrative |
| **Contagion Witness**     | Protagonist watches disease outrun containment                  | Helplessness; exponential threat; individual cases as synecdoche                       | The multiplying — one patient, then three, then a ward, then a street              |
| **Near-Miss / Exposure**  | Protagonist or ally personally exposed to pathogen              | Protagonist's own body becomes the stakes; time becomes visceral                       | Self-monitoring — am I warm? Is that a symptom? Body as unreliable narrator        |

## The Diagnosis Scene: 5-Beat Structure

Diagnosis is the medical thriller's equivalent of the detective's deduction scene. Follow this rhythm:

### Beat 1 — Anomalous Presentation

Something doesn't fit. A healthy person collapses. A disease appears in the wrong season, the wrong population, the wrong geography. This is the inciting mystery.

### Beat 2 — False Trail

The initial hypothesis is wrong. In a Song dynasty context, create two competing misreadings:

- The protagonist's modern epidemiological pattern-matching misleads because she's applying frameworks to a population with different baseline health, diet, and endemic disease profile
- Song physicians' Cold Damage framework is also wrong — but wrong in a different direction

Two competing misreadings create richer drama than a single wrong answer.

### Beat 3 — The Deeper Look

The protagonist goes beyond standard protocol. She examines what others ignore: the water source, food supply, travel history, timeline of symptom onset across households.

In a pre-modern setting, this is observation and deduction rather than lab work — more dramatic, not less, because the reader can follow every step.

### Beat 4 — The Recognition Moment

Connect two seemingly unrelated observations. The best diagnosis scenes produce an "of course" feeling — the clues were visible, but only the protagonist's framework could connect them.

### Beat 5 — The Implication

The diagnosis reveals something larger. Not just "this patient has X" but:

- "This means the water supply is contaminated"
- "This means the disease came with the exam candidates"
- "This means the military camp has weeks, not months"

**Critical constraint:** Without lab confirmation, the protagonist cannot verify her diagnosis. She can only predict — "if I'm right, then in three days we'll see these symptoms in these people." This transforms diagnosis from a moment of certainty into a bet. Diagnosis-as-prediction is inherently more dramatic than diagnosis-as-certainty.

## The Near-Miss / Exposure Scare

When the protagonist or a close ally is personally exposed:

- **The body becomes unreliable narrator.** Every sensation is potentially a symptom. Warmth could be fever or anxiety. A headache could be prodromal or stress. The protagonist applies her clinical knowledge to her own body and finds it terrifyingly ambiguous.
- **Time becomes visceral.** Incubation periods create a specific countdown. The protagonist knows exactly how many hours until she'd expect symptoms — and each hour without them is relief layered with dread.
- **Professional identity fractures.** The clinician who observes others must now observe herself. The shift from subject to object of medical attention is psychologically destabilizing.

## Autopsy / Examination Scenes

The dead body as text — reading it for information that changes the living narrative.

- **Discovery structure:** Normal finding, normal finding, normal finding — anomaly. The anomaly rewrites everything that came before.
- **The interior revealed:** Pre-modern autopsy is rare and socially transgressive (especially in Confucian culture). Use this transgression as dramatic pressure — the protagonist must justify violating bodily integrity.
- **Clinical precision is the right register.** Flat, controlled description. The horror comes from what is found, not from how it is described.

## Why Diagnosis-as-Prediction Beats Diagnosis-as-Certainty

In modern medical fiction, diagnosis ends with lab confirmation. In pre-modern fiction, the protagonist stakes her credibility on a prediction that hasn't been verified. This creates:

- **Sustained suspense** — the story doesn't resolve at diagnosis but extends through the verification window
- **Social stakes** — if she's wrong, she loses credibility for the next crisis
- **Moral weight** — she asks others to act on her unproven word, putting their resources and political capital at risk
- **Escalating investment** — each successful prediction builds social capital; each failure costs more because expectations are higher

## Making Disease Sensory: The Five Registers

### Sound

Primary vital sign without monitors. Labored breathing changes character as disease progresses — rapid and shallow to irregular to the death rattle. The sickroom soundscape includes other patients, caregivers, ambient city sounds outside. The contrast between normalcy out there and crisis in here.

### Smell

Historical fiction's advantage over modern settings. Without antiseptic, ventilation systems, or sealed waste disposal, the 1070s sickroom assaults the nose:

- Typhoid: distinctive sweet-musty odor
- Gangrene: rotting meat
- Dysentery: liquid stool
- Fever: acrid sweat
- Layered with incense and fumigation smoke (burning atractylodes root, standard Song practice)

### Touch

The protagonist's hands are her primary diagnostic instruments:

- Heat of fever through skin
- Texture of a rash — raised, smooth, weeping
- Hardness of a swollen lymph node
- Pulse quality (Song vocabulary: "floating," "sinking," "wiry," "slippery")
- The moment a body goes cold

### Sight

Skin color tells the disease's story: flushed to pale to gray to mottled purple (circulatory failure). Jaundice yellows the whites of the eyes before the skin. Dehydration sinks the eyes, hollows the cheeks. The sickroom itself: makeshift beds, piled linens, light or darkness, faces of caregivers.

### Taste / Visceral

The patient's own experience: metallic taste of blood, bitter nausea, dry mouth of dehydration, ache that lives in the bones during fever. When POV shifts to a patient or the protagonist falls ill, this register becomes primary.

## Three Rules for the Visceral/Gratuitous Line

**Visceral description is connected to character; gratuitous description is connected to spectacle.** If the reader understands the horror through a character's experience, it serves the story. If the horror exists for its own sake, it breaks trust.

1. **The one vivid detail rule.** Choose one precisely observed sensory detail; let the reader's imagination fill the rest. Preston's Ebola hemorrhage works because he selects specific images (bleeding eyes, "red vomit"), not because he describes everything.

2. **Emotional aftermath outweighs physical detail.** The protagonist's hands stopping. The change from frantic action to stillness. What she sees when she closes her eyes afterward. Spend more time here than on the medical event.

3. **Clinical precision > purple prose.** Medical vocabulary is more unsettling than flowery metaphors. A flat, controlled voice conveys horror more effectively than an overwrought one — it implies someone who has seen this before and is still shaken.
