# Epidemic Arc Structure

Rosenberg's 4-act epidemic dramaturgy and how to vary it across a multi-book series.

## The Four Acts in Detail

### Act I — Progressive Revelation / Denial (Inciting Incident)

Strange signs appear. Authorities minimize. A Cassandra figure notices patterns. Tension comes from dramatic irony: the reader knows more than most characters.

- Camus opens _The Plague_ with dead rats while officials refuse to name the disease
- Barry spends a full third of _The Great Influenza_ building the world of American medicine before the flu arrives — investing the reader in characters before crisis
- Preston opens _The Hot Zone_ with Charles Monet's infection and hemorrhagic death — establishing stakes through the body, not statistics

**For this series:** the protagonist recognizes a disease pattern that Song physicians interpret through their Cold Damage (shanghan) framework. She sees what's coming; they see something different. Both read the same evidence through incompatible lenses.

### Act II — Managing Randomness / Spread (Rising Action)

Disease is named. Authorities respond inadequately. Quarantine begins. Social fabric strains. Scapegoating emerges. Exponential spread creates a natural ticking clock.

King's Chapter 8 of _The Stand_ is the masterpiece: he traces infection vectors through named, individualized encounters — one soldier infects a gas station attendant, who infects a trucker, who infects a waitress. The "chain-letter structure" makes every human interaction a potential death sentence.

### Act III — Peak / Crisis (Climax)

Maximum death, maximum social breakdown, maximum moral testing. The question shifts from "will it spread?" to "can anyone intervene?"

Camus places his moral climax here — the agonizing death of a child forces characters to confront innocent suffering. Brooks structures her peak through worsening crises: plague deaths, then witch-hunting, then mob violence, then despair, then madness.

**The peak is not necessarily the highest body count — it is the point of greatest moral pressure.**

### Act IV — Subsidence / Aftermath (Resolution)

Rosenberg: epidemics "ordinarily end with a whimper, not a bang." The epidemic wanes gradually, often inexplicably. Retrospection and moral reckoning follow.

This phase is the most variable across reference works:

- _The Stand_ — two-thirds of its length here (aftermath as moral theater)
- _Station Eleven_ — the dominant register (aftermath as elegy)
- _The Plague_ — Rieux warns "the plague bacillus never dies" (aftermath as vigilance)
- _The Great Influenza_ — connects to institutional reform (aftermath as political legacy)

## Phase Compression/Expansion Table

The phase an author expands most reveals the story's true subject.

| Phase        | Preston       | King               | Camus        | Brooks       | Mandel        | Barry          |
| ------------ | ------------- | ------------------ | ------------ | ------------ | ------------- | -------------- |
| Pre-epidemic | Moderate      | Brief              | Detailed     | Brief frame  | Extensive     | Very extensive |
| First cases  | **Extensive** | Moderate           | Moderate     | Extended     | Nearly absent | Moderate       |
| Spread       | Compressed    | **Extended**       | Compressed   | **Extended** | Nearly absent | Extended       |
| Peak         | Extended      | Compressed         | **Extended** | Extended     | Nearly absent | Extended       |
| Intervention | **Extended**  | Minimal            | Extended     | Extended     | Nearly absent | Extended       |
| Aftermath    | Brief         | **Very extensive** | Moderate     | Brief        | **Dominant**  | Moderate       |

**Reading the table:** Preston's subject is the heroic scientist (expands intervention). King's subject is moral choice after collapse (expands aftermath). Camus's subject is endurance under absurd suffering (expands peak). Brooks's subject is community under pressure (expands spread).

## Varying Shape Across 6-8 Books

### Strategy 1: Different Phase Focus Per Book

Each novel weights a different Rosenberg act:

- Book 1 (Hangzhou urban epidemic) — expand **spread** phase. Discovery and horror in a dense city.
- Book 2 (military camp) — expand **peak**. Survival and containment under siege conditions.
- Book 4 (famine-linked disease) — expand **pre-epidemic**. Build social conditions that make the outbreak inevitable.
- Book 5 (exam system in Kaifeng) — expand **intervention**. Protagonist has learned enough to attempt systemic measures.

### Strategy 2: Different Geographic/Social Containers

Each setting creates different vectors, speeds, and social dynamics:

- Port city: disease moves along trade routes
- Military camp: sealed pressure cooker
- Famine zone: disease moves slowly through depleted populations
- Dense urban center: rapid spread, overwhelming density
- Rural network: slow spread across distances, isolated communities

### Strategy 3: Rotating Genre Tones

The epidemic provides the engine; the genre provides the chassis:

- Book 1: Medical detective story (identification)
- Book 2: Survival thriller (containment under duress)
- Book 3: Political thriller (institutional corruption meets waterborne disease)
- Book 4: Literary tragedy (slow devastation)
- Book 5: Procedural (implementing systematic measures)

### Strategy 4: Escalating Protagonist Capability

- Book 1: Unknown outsider — no credibility, no allies
- Mid-series: Has allies, enemies, reputation, trained apprentices
- Late series: New vulnerabilities from social capital — more to lose, more people depending on her, political enemies who want her to fail

### Strategy 5: Different Pathogens

Each pathogen has its own narrative rhythm:

- Fast respiratory illness: compressed arc, days not weeks
- Waterborne disease: slow-burn tied to infrastructure, investigation-driven
- Famine-linked disease: long pre-epidemic buildup of vulnerability
- Zoonotic outbreak: requires tracing the animal-human interface
- Endemic disease flaring: not new, but newly virulent — requires explaining why now
