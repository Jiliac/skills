# Anachronism Final Pass: Three-Pass Diagnostic Protocol

This reference provides a comprehensive method for detecting and eliminating anachronisms in historical fiction manuscripts. The three-pass method targets factual, tonal, and conceptual anachronisms in sequence.

## Why Three Separate Passes?

Each type of anachronism requires a different diagnostic lens:

- **Factual anachronisms** are objective (stirrups exist or don't exist in 200 BC)
- **Tonal anachronisms** are linguistic (detecting modern idioms requires language expertise)
- **Conceptual anachronisms** are worldview-based (detecting modern psychology requires understanding period-specific belief systems)

Attempting to check all three simultaneously leads to missing the subtle conceptual errors while fixating on obvious factual ones.

## Pass 1: Factual Anachronisms

### Definition

Factual anachronisms are physical objects, technologies, geographical features, or documented events that appear out of place in the designated timeline.

**Examples:**

- Stirrups in Roman cavalry (stirrups arrived in Europe ~600 AD)
- Tomatoes in medieval European cuisine (arrived from Americas post-1500)
- Characters sailing from a port that didn't exist yet
- References to a battle that occurred 10 years later than the story's timeframe

### Diagnostic Protocol

**Step 1: Material Culture Audit**

Scan the manuscript for every physical object mentioned:

- Clothing and textiles
- Weapons and armor
- Food and drink
- Architecture and buildings
- Transportation (ships, wagons, riding equipment)
- Writing materials (paper, parchment, ink)
- Currency and trade goods

**Step 2: Verify Each Item**

For items you're uncertain about, consult:

1. Primary sources from the era (if accessible)
2. Archaeological evidence
3. Specialized historical databases (material culture indices)
4. Museum catalogs from the period/region

**Warning:** Do NOT rely on Wikipedia or general historical overviews for material culture verification. These sources frequently conflate centuries and regions.

**Step 3: Geographical Verification**

Check:

- Place names (did this city exist yet? was it called this?)
- Geographical features (rivers change course, coastlines shift)
- Political boundaries (was this region part of this empire at this time?)
- Trade routes (was this commodity available in this location?)

**Step 4: Event Timeline Verification**

If your story references documented historical events:

- Verify dates precisely
- Confirm sequence of events
- Check whether your characters would realistically know about the event yet (information traveled slowly)

### Common Factual Anachronisms That Survive Drafts

**Food:**

- Potatoes, tomatoes, chocolate, coffee, tea in pre-Columbian Old World
- Sugar before medieval period (honey was the sweetener)
- Specific fruits/vegetables outside their native regions before trade

**Textiles:**

- Cotton before medieval trade routes established it
- Specific dye colors (purple was rare and expensive, synthetic dyes are modern)
- Button fastenings before 13th century Europe

**Weapons:**

- Crossbows before medieval period
- Specific metallurgy (steel types, Damascus steel myths)
- Gunpowder weapons before 13th/14th century

**Architecture:**

- Glass windows in lower-class dwellings
- Chimneys before late medieval period (smoke escaped through roof holes)
- Specific architectural styles centuries before their development

**Time and Measurement:**

- Clocks and precise timekeeping before mechanical clocks (~14th century)
- Standardized measurements before modern era
- Modern calendar references (characters wouldn't say "it's 63 BC")

### How to Fix Factual Anachronisms

**Option 1: Replace with period-accurate equivalent**

Anachronistic: "He stirred sugar into his tea."
Period-accurate (medieval): "He drizzled honey into his wine."

**Option 2: Cut entirely if not essential**

If the object serves no narrative purpose, delete the reference rather than researching a replacement.

**Option 3: Genericize**

If you're uncertain about specifics, use generic terms:

- "He sweetened the drink" (avoids naming the sweetener)
- "She fastened her cloak" (avoids specifying brooches vs. buttons vs. ties)

### Acceptable Factual Deviations

Not every factual deviation is worth correcting. Harris principle: Choose narrative drive over pedantic accuracy.

**When to let it go:**

- The anachronism is minor and obscure (only specialists would notice)
- Correcting it would require awkward explanation
- The deviation serves a major narrative purpose
- Historical records are ambiguous or contradictory anyway

**Example:** Shakespeare gave Bohemia a seacoast. Historically wrong, but the narrative required it. No one cares.

## Pass 2: Tonal and Linguistic Anachronisms

### Definition

Tonal anachronisms occur when language, idioms, metaphors, or narrative voice uses words, phrases, or rhetorical patterns that didn't exist in the designated period—or when the narrative adopts an inappropriate register (museum curator objectivity, modern slang).

**Examples:**

- "Survival of the fittest" before Darwin (1860s)
- "Achilles' heel" before the phrase entered English (19th century)
- "Clockwork precision" before mechanical clocks existed
- Modern corporate jargon ("leverage," "synergy," "impact" as verb)
- "Killed two birds with one stone" used in ancient Rome

### Diagnostic Protocol

**Step 1: Idiom and Metaphor Audit**

Scan for figurative language and verify:

- When did this phrase enter the language?
- What cultural/technological context produced it?
- Would a character from this era have access to that context?

**Resources:**

- Oxford English Dictionary (etymologies with first recorded use dates)
- Historical idiom databases
- Period-specific language references

**Step 2: Narrative Voice Audit**

Check whether the narrative voice:

- Adopts a "museum curator" objectivity explaining artifacts
- Uses modern journalistic language ("Meanwhile, across the city...")
- Employs modern psychological terminology in omniscient narration
- References the future or uses hindsight ("This would later become...")

**Step 3: Profanity and Vulgarity Check**

Profanity is period-specific and class-specific.

**Common mistakes:**

- Modern profanity ("fuck" is medieval but rare in formal writing; "shit" is ancient but usage varies)
- Wrong sacred references (swearing by wrong gods/saints for the region)
- Class-inappropriate vulgarity (aristocrats using peasant curses)

**Period-appropriate profanity:**

- Religious profanity is common (oaths by gods, saints, Christ's wounds)
- Scatological references exist but are class-specific
- Sexual profanity varies wildly by culture and era

### Common Tonal Anachronisms

**Corporate/Business Jargon:**

- "Leverage" (as verb), "synergy," "bandwidth," "circle back"
- "Move the needle," "game-changer," "low-hanging fruit"

These terms signal modern business culture and shatter immersion.

**Modern Idioms with Hidden Modern Origins:**

- "Cut to the chase" (early film editing)
- "Get your act together" (vaudeville theater)
- "Bite the bullet" (anesthesia-free battlefield surgery, 19th century)
- "Jump on the bandwagon" (American political campaigns, 19th century)

**Metaphors from Technologies That Don't Exist Yet:**

- "Like clockwork" (before mechanical clocks)
- "Firing on all cylinders" (internal combustion engines)
- "Carbon copy" (modern office technology)

**Modern Casual Speech:**

- "Okay," "yeah," "nope" (these are modern informal)
- "Guys" (modern informal group address)
- "Cool," "awesome," "totally" (modern slang)

### How to Fix Tonal Anachronisms

**Option 1: Use period-neutral equivalents**

Modern: "We need to leverage our assets."
Period-neutral: "We must use what we have."

Modern: "He was firing on all cylinders."
Period-neutral: "He was relentless." or "His energy was boundless."

**Option 2: Create period-appropriate metaphors**

Base metaphors on period-specific experiences:

- Agricultural metaphors (seasons, harvest, planting)
- Military metaphors (siege, garrison, fortification)
- Religious metaphors (pilgrimage, sacrament, divine will)
- Natural world metaphors (storms, rivers, animals)

**Example:**

Modern tech metaphor: "His mind worked like a well-oiled machine."
Period-appropriate: "His thoughts moved like cavalry—swift, precise, deadly."

**Option 3: Cut the metaphor entirely**

If you can't find a period-appropriate equivalent, use direct language:

Metaphorical: "We're running out of runway."
Direct: "We're running out of time."

### The "Museum Prose" Problem

Museum prose occurs when the narrative voice shifts from character perspective to objective historical explanation.

**Example of museum prose:**

> The atrium was a central feature of Roman domestic architecture. Originally deriving from Etruscan design, it served as the primary reception area for clients and visitors. The impluvium, a shallow pool in the floor, collected rainwater from the compluvium, an opening in the roof above.

This reads like a museum placard, not a thriller.

**Fix: Embed in character experience**

> Tiro crossed the atrium, his sandals slipping on the wet marble around the pool. Rain dripped from the opening above, echoing in the vast space. Cicero's clients huddled along the walls, waiting.

Now the architecture is experienced through action, not explained by a curator.

### The Hindsight Problem

Avoid narrative voice that references the future or frames events with modern historical judgment.

**Avoid:**

- "This decision would later prove catastrophic..." (characters don't know the future)
- "In what would become known as the Cataline Conspiracy..." (they didn't call it that yet)
- "Little did he know that..." (cheap dramatic irony, breaks POV)

**Fix: Stay in character POV and present moment**

Characters experience their present as chaotic and uncertain, not as a neat historical progression leading to known outcomes.

## Pass 3: Conceptual Anachronisms

### Definition

Conceptual anachronisms occur when modern social categories, political systems, psychological frameworks, or worldviews are imposed on historical characters who operated under entirely different belief systems.

These are the most insidious errors because they feel natural to modern writers and readers. They're also the hardest to detect because they require understanding the period's worldview from within.

### Why Conceptual Anachronisms Matter

Conceptual anachronisms falsify the historical mindset. They turn historical characters into modern people wearing historical costumes.

**Example:**

A medieval peasant thinking: "I'm trapped in an oppressive feudal system. If only we had democracy and individual rights."

This imposes modern political categories (feudalism, democracy, individual rights) onto a person who would have understood their world through divine order, Christian duty, and social estates ordained by God.

### The Three Categories of Conceptual Anachronism

#### 1. Modern Psychology and Selfhood

**Modern concepts that didn't exist:**

- "Finding myself" / "Being authentic" (modern individualism)
- "Processing emotions" / "Working through trauma" (therapeutic language)
- "Toxic relationship" / "Boundaries" / "Emotional labor" (modern psychology)
- "Self-esteem" / "Self-worth" (modern concept of self)

**Period-appropriate frameworks:**

- Honor/shame cultures (reputation in community eyes, not internal self-concept)
- Religious frameworks (sin, grace, divine will)
- Duty and obligation (to family, lord, god)
- Fate and fortune (external forces, not personal agency)

**Example:**

Modern psychology:

> "I need to set boundaries with my father. His expectations are damaging my mental health."

Period-appropriate (medieval):

> "I have failed my father. I have shamed our house. God has cursed me for my disobedience."

#### 2. Modern Political and Social Categories

**Modern concepts that didn't exist:**

- "Left/right" politics (French Revolution origin)
- "Liberal/conservative" (19th century)
- "Democracy" as a positive good (many eras saw it as mob rule)
- "Progress" as linear advancement (Enlightenment concept)
- "Human rights" (18th century Enlightenment)
- "Social justice" (modern framework)

**Period-appropriate frameworks:**

- Divine right vs. earthly corruption
- Order vs. chaos
- Virtue vs. vice (classical framework)
- Legitimate authority vs. tyranny
- God's will vs. human sin

**Example:**

Modern political categories:

> "The Senate represents an oligarchic elite oppressing the working class. We need democratic reforms to redistribute power."

Period-appropriate (Roman):

> "The Senate has abandoned virtue and serves only greed. The gods punish Rome for the corruption of our fathers. Only a return to ancestral custom can restore order."

#### 3. Modern Social Norms and Values

**Modern concepts:**

- Modern feminism without contextual grounding
- Modern concepts of childhood and parenting
- Modern privacy expectations
- Modern concepts of romantic love and marriage
- Modern work/life balance concepts

**Period-appropriate frameworks:**

- Marriage as political/economic alliance
- Children as economic assets / lineage continuation
- Public vs. private sphere (structured very differently)
- Different concepts of love (courtly love, duty, passion)
- Work as identity and social position

**Example:**

Modern social norms:

> "I married him for love, not for politics. I deserve happiness in my personal life."

Period-appropriate (medieval aristocrat):

> "My father arranged this marriage to secure our alliance with Burgundy. I have done my duty. Love may come, or it may not. That is God's will."

### Diagnostic Protocol for Conceptual Anachronisms

**Step 1: Character Thought Audit**

Read every instance of internal character thought (internal monologue, free indirect discourse, first-person narration).

**Ask:**

- Is this character using modern psychological language?
- Is this character framing their situation using modern political/social categories?
- Would a person from this era think about themselves and their world this way?

**Step 2: Motivation Audit**

**Ask:**

- What motivates this character?
- Are their motivations universal (fear, ambition, love, survival) or modern-specific (self-actualization, personal freedom, individual rights)?

**Universal motivations that transcend eras:**

- Physical survival and safety
- Protecting family/clan/tribe
- Achieving honor and avoiding shame
- Securing power and resources
- Love and desire (expression varies, emotion is universal)
- Religious devotion or fear of divine punishment

**Modern-specific motivations:**

- "Finding myself" / personal authenticity
- Work/life balance
- Personal fulfillment outside social role
- Individual rights and freedoms
- Romantic love as basis for marriage (modern Western concept)

**Step 3: Conflict Structure Audit**

How are conflicts framed?

**Modern framing:**

- Individual vs. oppressive system
- Personal freedom vs. societal expectations
- Self-fulfillment vs. duty

**Period-appropriate framing (varies by era):**

- Duty to family vs. duty to lord/king
- Divine will vs. human desire
- Honor vs. survival
- Legitimate authority vs. tyranny
- Order vs. chaos

### The "Vicious" vs. "Necessary" Anachronism Distinction

Cultural historian Ed Jonker distinguishes:

**Necessary conceptual anachronisms:** Terms we need to make history comprehensible

- "The Renaissance" (people living then didn't call it that)
- "Antiquity" (modern historical categorization)
- "Medieval" (imposed by later historians)

These structural terms aid reader comprehension without falsifying historical mindsets.

**Vicious conceptual anachronisms:** Imposing modern worldviews that falsify historical thinking

- Medieval characters thinking in terms of "progress" vs. "tradition"
- Ancient Romans framing politics as "left" vs. "right"
- Pre-Enlightenment characters valuing individual rights over social order

**Rule:** Use necessary anachronisms for structure, eliminate vicious anachronisms that falsify mindsets.

### How to Fix Conceptual Anachronisms

**Step 1: Research the period worldview**

Read primary sources to understand how people from this era thought about:

- The self and identity
- Social order and hierarchy
- Divine vs. human agency
- Gender roles and expectations
- Power and legitimacy

**Step 2: Reframe character thoughts**

Translate modern psychology into period-appropriate frameworks.

**Modern:**

> "I feel trapped in a toxic relationship with the Emperor. I need to prioritize my own mental health and set boundaries."

**Period-appropriate (Roman):**

> "The Emperor has become a tyrant. To remain in his service is to participate in vice. I must choose: honor or survival."

**Step 3: Retain emotional core, change expression**

The emotion is universal; the framework for understanding it is period-specific.

**Example: Grief**

Modern expression:

> "I'm going through the five stages of grief. I need time to process this loss."

Universal emotion, period expression (medieval):

> "God has taken her. I must submit to His will, though my heart rebels."

### Special Case: Progressive Values in Historical Characters

**The problem:** Writers want to create sympathetic characters with modern progressive values (feminism, anti-racism, class equality) in eras where those concepts didn't exist.

**Bad solution:** Impose modern values without explanation

> A medieval noblewoman thinking: "This feudal system oppresses women. We deserve equal rights and democratic representation."

This is pure conceptual anachronism.

**Better solution:** Create characters who are _unusual for their era_ but use period-appropriate reasoning

> "My father taught me Latin and rhetoric, as he had no sons. The Church says women are weak in mind, but I have read Cicero and Seneca. Are their words less true when I speak them?"

This character challenges gender norms using period-appropriate arguments (classical education, religious debate) rather than modern feminist theory.

**Best solution:** Show the tension between period norms and universal human desires

> "I am to marry the Duke of Burgundy. I am fifteen. I have no say. My brother chose his own bride, but I am property. This is the way of the world. But I do not have to accept it gladly."

This character experiences the injustice without possessing modern political language to name it as systemic oppression.

### Self-Audit Checklist for Conceptual Anachronisms

For each major character, ask:

**Psychological framework:**

- [ ] Do they think about themselves using modern therapeutic language?
- [ ] Do they value individual authenticity over social duty?
- [ ] Do they process emotions through modern psychological models?

**Political framework:**

- [ ] Do they use modern political categories (left/right, liberal/conservative)?
- [ ] Do they view history as progress toward modernity?
- [ ] Do they possess modern concepts of rights, democracy, or equality without contextual grounding?

**Social framework:**

- [ ] Do they expect modern privacy?
- [ ] Do they view marriage/family through modern romantic love paradigm?
- [ ] Do they value individual fulfillment over social role?

**Worldview:**

- [ ] Do they understand their era as primitive or backward (hindsight)?
- [ ] Do they possess knowledge or perspectives they couldn't have?
- [ ] Do they see themselves as agents of historical change using modern frameworks?

If you answer "yes" to any of these, you've likely got a conceptual anachronism to fix.

## Integration: The Three Passes in Sequence

**Pass 1 (Factual):** Objective, verifiable, fast

- Audit objects, events, geography
- Fix: Replace or cut

**Pass 2 (Tonal):** Linguistic, requires language expertise

- Audit idioms, metaphors, narrative voice
- Fix: Period-neutral equivalents or period-appropriate metaphors

**Pass 3 (Conceptual):** Worldview, requires understanding period belief systems

- Audit character thoughts, motivations, conflicts
- Fix: Reframe using period-appropriate frameworks while retaining emotional core

## When to Accept Anachronism

Historical fiction is not documentary. Some anachronisms are acceptable trade-offs.

**Accept anachronism when:**

1. **Readability demands it** - Obscure period-accurate term would confuse readers
2. **Narrative requires it** - The story structure demands minor deviation from history
3. **Universal emotions trump period expression** - Readers must emotionally connect with characters

**Harris principle:** "Extremely accurate and completely fantastical at the same time."

The story must always dominate the history lesson.

## Summary

**Goal:** Eliminate anachronisms that break immersion without sacrificing narrative drive.

**Method:** Three sequential passes targeting factual, tonal, and conceptual errors.

**Priority:** Conceptual anachronisms are the most dangerous because they falsify historical mindsets while feeling natural to modern writers.

**Test:** Would a person from this era think, speak, and act this way? If not, but the narrative demands it, is the trade-off worth it?
