# The Harris Anti-Patterns Checklist

Common historical fiction failures and how Robert Harris systematically avoids them. This reference identifies what NOT to do when balancing historical authenticity with thriller pacing.

## What Makes This "Harris-Specific"

Robert Harris has written multiple bestselling historical thrillers (the Cicero trilogy, _Fatherland_, _Pompeii_, _The Ghost_) that succeed as both thrillers and historically rigorous fiction. His work demonstrates how to avoid the common pitfalls that plague the genre.

This checklist extracts the anti-patterns Harris explicitly avoids based on his interviews, his published work, and developmental editing principles from the historical thriller genre.

## Anti-Pattern 1: The "Documentary" Trap

### Definition

Prioritizing documentary fidelity to historical detail at the complete expense of narrative drive. The writer becomes paralyzed by pedantic accuracy, slowing the story to ensure every detail is perfectly researched and explained.

### Why It Fails

Readers pick up historical thrillers for the thriller, not for a history textbook. If they wanted pure documentary truth, they'd read nonfiction.

### Harris's Approach

Harris explicitly states his books are "both extremely accurate and completely fantastical at the same time." He researches exhaustively (reading 29 volumes of Cicero for the trilogy) but then **sets the research aside** and writes the story.

**Harris principle:** Know ten times more than you'll use. Use only what serves the story.

### How to Avoid

**Test:** When choosing between correcting a minor historical inaccuracy and maintaining narrative momentum, choose momentum.

**Example scenarios:**

**Documentary trap:**

> Stop the narrative to explain the precise constitutional procedures of the Roman Senate, complete with footnotes and historical asides, even though it slows the thriller pacing to a crawl.

**Harris approach:**

> Embed the essential constitutional detail in conflict ("You can't convene the Senate without a quorum—and Crassus controls twenty votes"), ignore the rest, and keep the story moving.

**When to let historical accuracy slide:**

- The deviation is minor and obscure (only specialists would notice)
- Correcting it requires stopping the narrative for explanation
- The story requires the deviation for thriller structure
- Historical records are contradictory anyway

**When NOT to let it slide:**

- The deviation breaks immersion for general readers
- It contradicts well-known historical facts
- It undermines the period setting entirely

## Anti-Pattern 2: The "Passive Observer" Protagonist

### Definition

The protagonist functions as a camera, wandering through historical events to witness them and report them to the reader, but never actively driving the plot.

### Why It Fails

Passive protagonists kill thriller tension. Thrillers require active protagonists with urgent goals who face escalating obstacles.

### Harris's Solution: The "Tiro" Technique

Harris's signature innovation is the observer-narrator who is NOT the Great Man of history but is **always in the room** when history happens.

**Tiro** is Cicero's secretary in the Roman trilogy. He is:

- Close to power but not powerful himself
- Privy to conspiracies and political machinations
- An active participant (secretary, investigator, problem-solver)
- Able to explain events naturally (his job is to document and assist)

**Key:** Tiro is not passive. He actively investigates, manipulates, negotiates, and survives. The historical events happen around him, but he drives his own micro-narrative of survival and service.

### How to Avoid

**Test:** Does your protagonist have an urgent, specific goal that puts them in active conflict with their environment?

**Passive observer (bad):**

> Marcus wanders through Rome, witnessing the Catiline conspiracy unfold. He observes conversations, attends Senate meetings, and documents events. He has no personal stakes and takes no actions that affect outcomes.

**Active participant (Harris approach):**

> Tiro must investigate the conspiracy to protect Cicero's life. He interrogates witnesses, decodes encrypted messages, and manipulates informants. The conspiracy threatens him directly, and his actions (though not world-changing) affect his and Cicero's survival.

**Questions to ask:**

- Does my protagonist have a specific, urgent goal?
- Are they taking actions to achieve that goal?
- Do those actions create new obstacles and complications?
- Are they in personal danger if they fail?

If the answer to any of these is "no," you've got a passive observer, not a thriller protagonist.

## Anti-Pattern 3: The "Omniscient Historian" Voice

### Definition

The narrative voice steps back to explain the long-term historical significance of events with the benefit of hindsight. Characters recognize their era as a stepping stone to the modern world or comment on the "lessons of history."

### Why It Fails

It breaks immersion. Characters cannot know the future. To them, their present is chaotic, uncertain, and absolute. They don't see themselves as historical figures; they see themselves as people trying to survive.

### How It Manifests

**Common omniscient historian mistakes:**

- "This decision would later prove catastrophic..."
- "Little did he know that..."
- "In what would become known as the Catiline Conspiracy..."
- Characters discussing how "future historians will judge us"

**Conceptual version:**

- Characters understanding their era as "backward" or "primitive"
- Characters recognizing they're living through a "transition period" to modernity
- Characters possessing modern historical frameworks to categorize their own era

### Harris's Approach

Characters experience their present as the only reality. They operate with limited information, period-specific assumptions, and no knowledge of outcomes.

**Example:**

**Omniscient historian (bad):**

> "As Cicero spoke, he could not have known that this Senate speech would be remembered for two thousand years as one of the greatest orations in Western civilization."

**Harris approach (character present-tense experience):**

> Cicero gripped the rail. His voice echoed across the Senate floor. Would it be enough? Would they vote with him or destroy him?

The character experiences the moment as uncertain and dangerous, not as a neat historical milestone.

### How to Avoid

**Test:** Does the narrative voice reference the future, use hindsight, or frame events with modern historical categorizations?

**Fix:** Stay in the character's POV and present moment.

**Questions to ask:**

- Does my narrator explain what will happen later?
- Do characters possess knowledge they couldn't have?
- Do characters see themselves as historical figures rather than people in crisis?

## Anti-Pattern 4: The "Museum Prose" Problem

### Definition

The narrative voice shifts from character perspective to objective museum curator, explaining artifacts, architecture, customs, or politics with detached academic authority.

### Why It Fails

Museum prose destroys thriller pacing. It's emotionally distant, lacks urgency, and signals to the reader: "You are learning history now, not reading a thriller."

### How It Manifests

**Example:**

> The Roman atrium was a central architectural feature derived from Etruscan design. It served as the primary reception area for clients and visitors. The impluvium, a shallow pool in the floor, collected rainwater from the compluvium, an opening in the roof above. This ingenious system provided fresh water for the household while creating a visually impressive space that demonstrated the owner's wealth and status.

This reads like a museum placard, not a thriller.

### Harris's Approach: Discovery Through Action

Harris embeds historical detail in character action, sensory experience, and conflict. The reader learns about the atrium by experiencing it through Tiro's immediate crisis.

**Example:**

> Tiro crossed the atrium, his sandals slipping on the wet marble. Rain dripped through the opening above, echoing in the vast space. Cicero's clients pressed against the walls—twenty, thirty of them, all waiting. All watching.

Now the atrium is experienced through action and tension, not explained by a curator.

### How to Avoid

**Test:** Does the narrative voice shift to objective, educational explanation?

**Fix:** Embed the detail in character experience—action, sensory perception, dialogue, internal thought.

**Questions to ask:**

- Can I cut this explanation entirely without losing the scene?
- Can I embed this information in dialogue driven by conflict?
- Can I show this through character action instead of narrator explanation?

**Hierarchy of solutions:**

1. **Cut it** - Often the explanation isn't necessary at all
2. **Embed in dialogue** - Characters argue over the significance, not the facts
3. **Embed in action** - Character interacts with the environment
4. **Embed in internal thought** - POV character notices details relevant to their immediate problem

## Anti-Pattern 5: Research-Flexing

### Definition

The author showcases their exhaustive research on the page, regardless of relevance to plot or character. After spending months reading primary sources, the writer feels compelled to demonstrate that labor.

### Why It Fails

Research-flexing is the author intruding into the narrative to say, "Look how much I know!" It breaks POV, slows pacing, and shifts focus from the story to the author's credentials.

### Harris's Approach: The Hemingway Iceberg

Harris conducts years of research—but 90% of it stays underwater, invisible to the reader. Only the tip of the iceberg (the details that serve the immediate story) appears on the page.

**Harris principle:** Research deeply, show sparingly.

### How It Manifests

**Example:**

> Cicero entered the Forum. The Forum Romanum was the political and commercial heart of Rome, featuring the Rostra (speaker's platform), the Temple of Saturn (housing the state treasury), the Basilica Aemilia (for judicial proceedings), and the Curia (Senate house). Originally a marshy valley between the Palatine and Capitoline hills, it had been drained in the 6th century BC and paved over centuries of development.

This paragraph screams: "I read a book about the Roman Forum and I want you to know it."

**Harris approach:**

> Cicero pushed through the Forum crowds. The Rostra was already packed—senators, clients, hangers-on. No sign of Crassus. Where was he?

The Forum is a space for action and tension, not an architecture lecture.

### How to Avoid

**Test (The Tactical Relevance Test):**

For every historical detail, ask: Does the protagonist need this information to solve their current problem, survive a threat, or manipulate an opponent?

- If **yes**: Include it, embedded in action/conflict
- If **no**: Cut it

**Questions to ask:**

- Am I showing off knowledge or serving the story?
- Does this detail advance plot or reveal character?
- Would the scene work without this information?

**Acceptable ratio:** In a thriller, ~90% of text should drive plot/character conflict, ~10% establishes historical environment.

## Anti-Pattern 6: Expository Dialogue

### Definition

Characters deliver long, uninterrupted speeches explaining historical context, politics, or events to each other (and really to the reader). This is the "As you know, Bob" problem.

### Why It Fails

Real people don't lecture each other about things they both already know. Expository dialogue is unnatural, slows pacing, and signals: "The author needs you to know this."

### Harris's Approach: Embedding Exposition in Conflict

When Harris needs to convey historical context, he embeds it in argument, interrogation, or manipulation. Characters don't cooperatively explain things—they fight over what facts mean.

**Example:**

**Expository dialogue (bad):**

> "As you know, Cicero, the grain subsidies were first instituted by Gaius Gracchus to secure plebeian support. The Senate has always opposed them because they reduce patrician control over food distribution."
>
> "Yes, and that's why we must proceed carefully."

This is two characters lecturing each other (and the reader).

**Harris approach (conflict-driven exposition):**

> "The grain subsidies—"
>
> "I know the history, Cicero." Crassus cut him off. "Gaius Gracchus bought the mob. We won't make the same mistake."
>
> "It's not a mistake if it keeps us alive."

Now the historical fact (Gracchus, grain subsidies) emerges through conflict about what it means and what to do.

### How to Avoid

**Test:** Are characters cooperatively explaining things to each other?

**Fix:** Turn explanation into argument. Characters should debate significance, not recite facts.

**The "Tiro" Technique:**

- Tiro (observer-narrator) asks questions as part of his job
- Powerful characters answer with their own agenda (manipulation, testing loyalty)
- Exposition emerges as byproduct of interrogation

**Example:**

> "Why does Crassus control the grain contracts?" I asked.
>
> Cicero's smile was thin. "Because starving the plebs is how you win elections. Or lose your head."

The exposition (grain contracts = political leverage) is delivered as character insight, not academic lecture.

## Anti-Pattern 7: Dead Zone Chronicling

### Definition

The writer feels obligated to chronicle every historical period in chronological order, including months or years of uneventful transitions, travel, or waiting.

### Why It Fails

Thrillers demand compression. If nothing dramatically significant happens during a period, it should be summarized or skipped entirely.

### Harris's Approach: Ruthless Time Compression

Harris aggressively summarizes or jumps over historical dead zones. If a period lacks thriller stakes, it gets compressed into a single transitional sentence or eliminated.

**Example:**

**Dead zone chronicling (bad):**

> The winter passed slowly. Cicero attended Senate meetings, reviewed legal cases, and maintained correspondence with provincial governors. By March, the political situation remained stable. In April, rumors began circulating about Catiline.

This chronicles four months of nothing happening.

**Harris approach (compression):**

> The conspiracy went quiet for months. Then, in late spring, Catiline reappeared.

Dead time is compressed to a single sentence. We jump straight to the next thriller beat.

### How to Avoid

**Test:** Does this period contain a major plot event, character conflict, or escalation of stakes?

- If **yes**: Dramatize it as a full scene
- If **no**: Summarize in one sentence or skip entirely

**Questions to ask:**

- What changes during this period?
- Can I convey that change in summary rather than dramatized scenes?
- Does the thriller structure require me to show this, or can I jump ahead?

## Anti-Pattern 8: Over-Formalization After Authenticity Review

### Definition

In pursuit of period authenticity, the writer makes dialogue and prose so formal, archaic, or stilted that it loses the modern urgency established during thriller revision.

### Why It Fails

Authenticity review (Skill 5) comes AFTER thriller revision (Skill 4). If authenticity review strips away all the modern urgency, the manuscript stops working as a thriller.

### The Balance

Harris's dialogue is:

- **Natural** (human speech rhythms, not stilted constructions)
- **Period-appropriate** (era-specific vocabulary, no modern slang)
- **Urgent** (every line advances conflict, creates tension)

**Over-formalization breaks the third element** (urgency).

### How It Manifests

**Example:**

**After thriller revision (Skill 4):**

> "The Senate will tear us apart."
>
> "Then we move first."

**After over-aggressive authenticity review:**

> "The esteemed senators shall not, I fear, look with favor upon our proposed course of action."
>
> "Then we must endeavor to act with all due haste, lest they preempt our designs."

The second version is more "period-sounding" but has lost all thriller urgency.

### Harris's Balance

**Example:**

> "The Senate will destroy us."
>
> Cicero leaned closer. "Unless we strike first."

Natural syntax + period-neutral vocabulary + thriller urgency.

### How to Avoid

**Test:** Read dialogue aloud. Does it sound like people under pressure or like a Renaissance Faire?

**Fix:** Use the Hilary Mantel "sideways shift"—modern syntax with 1-2 period-specific words to suggest otherness.

**Questions to ask:**

- Have I made the dialogue so formal it's lost tension?
- Would a modern actor struggle to deliver this naturally?
- Am I prioritizing "sounding historical" over "creating suspense"?

**Rule:** When authenticity and thriller tension conflict, lean toward thriller tension. Minor anachronisms are acceptable if they preserve narrative drive.

## Anti-Pattern 9: The White Room (Over-Cutting for Pace)

### Definition

In an aggressive attempt to cut research-flexing and maintain thriller velocity, the editor strips away ALL descriptive grounding, leaving characters speaking in a featureless void.

### Why It Fails

Historical fiction requires period texture to create immersion. Without sensory anchors, the setting disappears entirely, and characters feel like modern people in historical costumes.

### The Balance

**Cut:** Paragraphs of architectural history, detailed explanations of customs, research-flexing
**Retain:** Sensory anchors (smell, temperature, texture, light, sound)

### How to Avoid

**Test:** Can the reader visualize the setting?

**Fix:** Retain 1-2 micro-details per scene that ground the action in period-specific sensory experience.

**Example:**

**White Room (over-cut):**

> "We need to move now."
>
> "Agreed."
>
> They left.

No setting. No texture. Pure dialogue void.

**Harris balance (sensory anchors + pace):**

> "We need to move now."
>
> Cicero pushed through the door. Outside, the Forum stank of fish and sweat. Noon sun blazed off marble.
>
> "Agreed."

Minimal detail (smell, temperature, light) grounds the scene without slowing pace.

**Target:** 1-2 sensory micro-details per scene, embedded in action beats, not separate paragraphs.

## Anti-Pattern 10: Hindsight Undermining Tension

### Definition

The reader knows the macro-historical outcome (Mount Vesuvius will erupt, Caesar will be assassinated, the regime will fall). The writer assumes this knowledge kills all suspense.

### Why It's Not Actually a Problem

Harris demonstrates that readers' knowledge of macro-history doesn't kill thriller tension—because the thriller tension comes from the **micro-narrative** (the specific fate of the fictional or lesser-known protagonist).

### Harris's Approach

**Example: _Pompeii_**

Every reader knows Vesuvius will erupt. Harris doesn't hide this—he uses it.

**Macro-history (known):** Vesuvius erupts in 79 AD
**Micro-narrative (unknown):** Will the protagonist (a water engineer) figure out the warning signs in time? Will he save the woman he loves? Will he survive?

The thriller tension comes from the protagonist's specific survival, not from whether the eruption happens.

### How to Apply

**Test:** Does my protagonist have a personal, specific goal independent of the known historical outcome?

**Fix:** Make the micro-narrative (protagonist's survival, love, redemption, revenge) the source of suspense, not the macro-history.

**Questions to ask:**

- What is at stake for my protagonist personally?
- Does the reader care about their specific fate even if they know the historical outcome?
- Am I using historical inevitability to create dread and urgency?

**Example: Caesar's assassination**

**Macro-history (known):** Caesar will be assassinated on the Ides of March
**Micro-narrative (unknown):** Will Tiro uncover the conspiracy in time to warn Cicero? Will Cicero get implicated? Will Tiro survive the purge that follows?

The known outcome creates dread ("it's coming"), but the micro-narrative creates suspense ("will they survive it?").

## Summary: The Harris Method

**What Harris does:**

1. Researches exhaustively, uses sparingly (iceberg principle)
2. Creates active protagonists with urgent personal stakes (Tiro technique)
3. Stays in character POV and present moment (no omniscient historian)
4. Embeds historical detail in action and conflict (no museum prose)
5. Cuts research-flexing ruthlessly (tactical relevance test)
6. Embeds exposition in argument and interrogation (no cooperative lectures)
7. Compresses dead zones aggressively (skip boring history)
8. Balances authenticity with thriller urgency (natural + period + urgent)
9. Retains sensory anchors while cutting bloat (texture without drag)
10. Uses known macro-history to create dread while focusing suspense on unknown micro-narrative

**What Harris avoids:**

1. Documentary pedantry over narrative drive
2. Passive observer protagonists
3. Omniscient historian voice with hindsight
4. Museum prose and academic explanations
5. Research-flexing and showing off knowledge
6. Expository dialogue and info-dumps
7. Chronicling historical dead zones
8. Over-formalization that kills thriller urgency
9. Over-cutting that creates white room syndrome
10. Assuming readers' historical knowledge kills suspense

## Using This Checklist

**After completing the two-pass authenticity review (dialogue + anachronism sweep), audit the manuscript against these ten anti-patterns.**

If you find yourself falling into any of these traps, the fix is usually one of:

1. **Cut** - Delete the offending passage entirely
2. **Embed** - Move information into dialogue, action, or internal thought
3. **Compress** - Summarize in one sentence instead of full scene
4. **Reframe** - Stay in character POV instead of omniscient explanation

**Final principle:** When authenticity and thriller tension conflict, lean toward thriller tension. Minor historical deviations are acceptable if they serve narrative drive.

Harris's books are "both extremely accurate and completely fantastical at the same time" because he knows when to prioritize the story over pedantic historical correctness.
