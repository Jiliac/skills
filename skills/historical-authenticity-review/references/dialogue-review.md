# Dialogue Authenticity Review Protocol

This reference provides a line-by-line audit protocol for reviewing historical fiction dialogue after thriller revision. The goal: period-appropriate speech that maintains modern thriller tension.

## The Central Tension

**Thriller revision (Skill 4)** injected modern urgency into dialogue, making politics feel immediate and vital.

**Authenticity review (Skill 5)** ensures that urgency doesn't break period immersion through modern slang, anachronistic concepts, or emotional patterns.

## The Harris Balance: Natural + Period + Urgent

Robert Harris's dialogue achieves three qualities simultaneously:

1. **Natural speech rhythms** - Characters speak like humans under pressure, not museum docents
2. **Period-appropriate vocabulary** - Era-specific nouns, verbs, and cultural references
3. **Thriller tension** - Every line advances conflict, reveals stakes, or creates suspense

### Example: The Three Levels

**Too Modern (breaks immersion):**

> "The Senate will totally freak out if we push this through. We need to lobby the key players."

**Too Stilted (museum prose):**

> "Prithee, the Senate shall not brook such perfidy. We must forthwith petition the most esteemed senators."

**Harris Balance (natural + period + urgent):**

> "The Senate will tear us apart." He leaned closer. "Unless we get to Crassus first."

## Diagnostic Protocol: Line-by-Line Audit

### Question 1: Modern Idioms and Slang

**Scan for:**

- Corporate jargon ("leverage," "synergy," "impact" as verb)
- Modern idioms ("throw under the bus," "on the same page")
- Contemporary slang ("cool," "guys," "totally")
- Modern profanity ("screw this," "bullshit")

**Fix:** Replace with period-neutral equivalents or cut entirely.

**Example:**

- Modern: "We need to get on the same page about this."
- Period-neutral: "We must agree on this."

### Question 2: Overly Long Exposition Speeches

**Diagnostic:** Does any character speak uninterrupted for 3+ sentences explaining historical context?

Characters in thrillers are combative and reactive. Long speeches signal info-dumping, not dialogue.

**Fix:** Break speeches with:

- Interruptions from other characters
- Action beats (character movement, physical reactions)
- Internal thoughts from POV character
- Strategic silences

**Example:**

**Before (exposition dump):**

> "The grain subsidies have been a cornerstone of Roman politics for three generations. They were first instituted by Gaius Gracchus to secure plebeian support. The Senate has always opposed them because they reduce patrician power over food distribution."

**After (combat dialogue):**

> "The grain subsidies—"
>
> "I know the history, Cicero." Crassus waved a dismissive hand.
>
> "Then you know the Senate will never agree."

### Question 3: Archaic Pastiche

**Scan for:**

- "Thee," "thou," "prithee," "forsooth"
- Inverted syntax ("Said he to me," "Know you not")
- Fake-formal constructions ("I beseech thee")

**The Hilary Mantel Technique: "Sideways Shift"**

Write in modern English, then shift it slightly "sideways" to suggest period difference without impeding readability.

**Method:**

1. Write the line in completely natural modern English
2. Replace 1-2 words with period-specific terms
3. Adjust sentence rhythm slightly (longer pauses, more formal address)
4. Test: Read aloud. If you stumble, it's too archaic.

**Example:**

**Step 1 (modern baseline):**

> "The king won't like this."

**Step 2 (sideways shift - period vocabulary):**

> "His Majesty will not tolerate this."

**Step 3 (sideways shift - rhythm adjustment):**

> "His Majesty will not tolerate it." A pause. "You know this."

### Question 4: Thriller Tension Test

**Diagnostic:** Does this dialogue create suspense, reveal stakes, or advance conflict?

If dialogue is purely informational or pleasantries, it fails the thriller test.

**Tension mechanics:**

- Characters deflect, lie, or obscure truth
- Subtext contradicts surface politeness
- Silences create pressure
- Questions go unanswered or answered with questions
- Power dynamics shift line-by-line

**Example:**

**No tension (informational):**

> "The emperor has returned from Gaul."
>
> "Yes, I heard. How was the campaign?"
>
> "Successful, I believe."

**Thriller tension (combat + subtext):**

> "The emperor has returned."
>
> Silence. Cato studied his wine.
>
> "You know what this means," I said.
>
> "I know what you think it means."

### Question 5: Emotional Anachronism

**Scan for modern psychological processing:**

- Therapy language ("I need to process this," "set boundaries")
- Modern concepts of selfhood ("finding myself," "being authentic")
- Contemporary emotional frameworks ("toxic relationship," "gaslighting")

**Period-appropriate emotions:**

Characters experience universal emotions (fear, ambition, love, grief) but express them through period-specific frameworks:

- Religious language for internal conflict
- Honor/shame rather than self-esteem
- Duty to family/clan rather than individual fulfillment
- Fate/fortune rather than personal agency

**Example:**

**Modern psychology:**

> "I need space to figure out who I am outside of this relationship."

**Period-appropriate:**

> "The gods have not shown me the path. I must seek their will."

Or, more secular:

> "I have a duty to my house. This alliance dishonors us."

## Reading Aloud as Diagnostic Tool

The most effective authenticity check: Read dialogue aloud at normal speaking pace.

**Listen for:**

- Do you stumble over archaic constructions?
- Does it sound like a lecture or a conversation?
- Can you hear the tension and urgency?
- Would a modern actor be able to deliver this naturally?

If the dialogue feels theatrical, stilted, or requires "acting" to make it work, it needs revision.

## The Action Beat Balance

Dialogue must be grounded in physical space without slowing pace.

**Target ratio:** 1 action beat per 3-4 exchanges.

**Action beats serve three functions:**

1. Identify speakers without dialogue tags
2. Ground conversation in period-specific environment
3. Convey emotional states through body language

**Example:**

> "The Senate will vote tomorrow."
>
> Cato set down his cup, the bronze rim catching firelight. "And you think they'll support you?"
>
> "I think they'll support gold."
>
> "Then you don't know senators."

The action beat (setting down cup, bronze detail, firelight) provides:

- Speaker identification (Cato)
- Period texture (bronze cup, firelight)
- Emotional subtext (deliberate, controlled movement = weighing options)

## Common Dialogue Failures Post-Thriller-Revision

### Failure 1: Overcorrection to Modern Urgency

**Symptom:** Characters sound like modern politicians or corporate executives.

**Example:**

> "We need to leverage Pompey's military assets to maximize Senate buy-in."

**Fix:** Maintain urgency but use period-neutral language.

> "We need Pompey's legions. Without them, the Senate will destroy us."

### Failure 2: Explaining Instead of Arguing

**Symptom:** Characters recite historical facts to each other instead of debating their significance.

**Fix:** Embed information in conflict. Characters should argue over what facts MEAN, not state them cooperatively.

**Before:**

> "The Gracchi brothers tried land reform and were both killed."
>
> "Yes, and that's why we should be careful."

**After:**

> "The Gracchi tried this."
>
> "The Gracchi were fools. We are not."

### Failure 3: Polite Exposition Between Enemies

**Symptom:** Political rivals have courteous, cooperative conversations.

**Reality:** Etiquette is weaponized. Surface politeness conceals lethal subtext.

**Example:**

**Too polite:**

> "Senator, I respectfully disagree with your position on the grain law."
>
> "I understand your concerns, but I believe my approach is sound."

**Weaponized courtesy:**

> "Senator, your... generosity... with the grain subsidies is noted."
>
> A thin smile. "As is your family's recent acquisition of Campanian wheat fields."

## Stilted Phrases That Survive First Drafts

These constructions feel period-appropriate but actually slow dialogue and break natural speech:

**Avoid:**

- "I do believe that..." → "I believe..."
- "It is my understanding that..." → "I understand..." or just state the fact
- "Might I suggest..." → "We should..." or direct imperative
- "One must consider..." → "Consider..." or use active voice
- "It would seem..." → "It seems..." or commit to the statement

**Exception:** Stilted formality is acceptable when:

- Character is deliberately using formal register as power move
- Diplomatic speech where protocol demands it
- Character is nervous and defaulting to over-formality

In these cases, stilted speech conveys character state, not authorial awkwardness.

## Class-Appropriate Speech Patterns

Different social classes speak differently, even within the same historical period.

**Aristocrats/Educated Elite:**

- Longer sentences, more complex syntax
- Classical allusions and literary references
- Indirect, euphemistic language for vulgar topics
- Formal address even in private

**Merchants/Middle Class:**

- Direct, transactional language
- Practical metaphors (trade, crafts, money)
- Less formal but still respectful address
- Blunt about money, indirect about politics

**Lower Class/Servants:**

- Short, declarative sentences
- Concrete metaphors from daily life
- Deferential address ("my lord," "sir")
- Indirect communication when speaking to superiors

**Soldiers:**

- Military metaphors
- Blunt, profane language among peers
- Formal discipline when addressing commanders
- Gallows humor

### Cross-Class Dialogue

Power dynamics are most visible when characters from different classes interact.

**Example: Aristocrat interviewing servant**

> "You saw the man who entered the house?"
>
> "Yes, my lord."
>
> "Describe him."
>
> "Tall, my lord. Dark cloak. Walked like... like quality, if you take my meaning."

The servant's speech:

- Short, deferential responses
- Repeated "my lord" signals status gap
- Concrete observation ("tall," "dark cloak")
- Hesitation before implying the man was nobility ("like quality")

## The Subtext Layer

Historical characters operating in dangerous political environments rarely say what they mean directly.

**Surface text:** Polite conversation about grain shipments
**Subtext:** Starving a rival's voting base to weaken their faction

The POV character's internal thoughts decode the threat for the reader.

**Example:**

> "I understand you've secured the Sicilian grain contracts," Caesar said pleasantly.
>
> _Which meant he knew exactly how much leverage I had with the urban plebs._
>
> "A fortunate negotiation," I replied.
>
> "Fortune indeed." He smiled. "One wonders what such fortune might cost. In the long term."
>
> _A threat. Comply or watch those contracts disappear._

The dialogue is courteous. The internal thoughts reveal it's combat.

## Final Authenticity Checklist

Before finalizing dialogue:

- [ ] Read aloud - Does it sound natural or stilted?
- [ ] Modern scan - Any corporate jargon, modern slang, or contemporary idioms?
- [ ] Archaic scan - Any "thee/thou" pastiche or inverted syntax?
- [ ] Tension test - Does every exchange advance conflict or reveal stakes?
- [ ] Emotional anachronism check - Any modern psychology or therapy language?
- [ ] Class-appropriate - Do characters speak according to their social position?
- [ ] Subtext layer - Is dangerous information conveyed indirectly?
- [ ] Action beat ratio - 1 grounding beat per 3-4 exchanges?
- [ ] Length check - Any uninterrupted 3+ sentence exposition speeches?

## When to Choose Readability Over Authenticity

**Rule:** If period-accurate language requires reader to stop and puzzle out meaning, choose clarity.

**Examples where clarity wins:**

- Obscure period-specific measurements → use approximate modern equivalents
- Impenetrable technical jargon → simplify or cut
- Complex honorific systems → streamline to essential distinctions

**Harris principle:** "Extremely accurate and completely fantastical at the same time."

The reader should never notice they're learning history. The story must always dominate.

## Integration with Thriller Revision

This dialogue review happens AFTER modern analog filter (Skill 4). The sequence matters:

1. **Draft:** Natural period dialogue (Skill 3)
2. **Thriller revision:** Inject modern urgency, cut info-dumps (Skill 4)
3. **Authenticity review:** Ensure urgency didn't break period immersion (Skill 5)

The two revision passes work in tension, not harmony. Modern analog filter pushes toward contemporary urgency; dialogue authenticity pulls back toward period accuracy. The final product balances both.

## Summary

**Goal:** Period-appropriate dialogue that maintains thriller tension.

**Method:** Line-by-line audit using diagnostic questions.

**Balance:** Natural speech rhythms + period vocabulary + thriller urgency.

**Test:** Read aloud. If you stumble, it's too archaic. If it sounds like a boardroom, it's too modern. If it sounds like a tense conversation between people in danger, it's right.
