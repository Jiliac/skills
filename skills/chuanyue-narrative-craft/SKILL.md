# Chuanyue Narrative Craft

Craft framework for transmigration fiction that treats modern knowledge as a constrained, depletable resource creating as many problems as it solves. This single principle separates literary-quality work from wish fulfillment.

## When to Use

- Design knowledge-deployment arcs across a novel or series
- Draft scenes where the protagonist applies modern expertise in a historical setting
- Handle collisions between modern science and historical knowledge systems
- Write fish-out-of-water orientation sequences
- Audit transmigration drafts for common failure modes
- Balance kingdom-building satisfaction with literary depth

## Core Principle

**Modern knowledge is currency, not lecture material.** It is spent, hoarded, rationed, and negotiated — never dumped. Every deployment must cost something: social capital, political exposure, personal risk, or the exhaustion of a finite knowledge reserve. The protagonist knows _about_ technologies but cannot build them without supply chains, materials, institutions, and local expertise she does not possess.

Assign the protagonist a specific professional background and stay within it. Readers accept deep expertise in one domain but rebel against omniscience.

## Knowledge Deployment Framework

Three named techniques form the structural core. Use them in combination, not isolation.

### Padway Ladder (dependency-chained innovation)

Structure knowledge deployment as a chain where each innovation creates the prerequisite for the next. Prevent the "magic wand" problem by making each step logically dependent on the previous one. The reader experiences forward-pulling tension from anticipating _what comes next_.

### Anderson Correction (knowledge failure as realism)

Knowing about a technology is not the same as building it from scratch. Deploy knowledge failures early and often — the protagonist's competence is system-dependent, and the system does not exist yet. Failed knowledge attempts build credibility for later successes.

### Eclipse Gambit (credibility-establishing demonstration)

One verifiable, time-sensitive piece of knowledge deployed in a single dramatic scene creates a "credibility bank" the protagonist draws on for subsequent, slower work. Use once in the first act, then shift to less spectacular modes of influence. Repetition turns it into a parlor trick.

### Social Friction Engine (political cost of success)

Every successful innovation creates political enemies. Treat this as automatic — no improvement goes unpunished. A better irrigation system threatens the water-rights holder; a new medical protocol undermines the guild physician. Build the opposition into the Padway Ladder itself.

### Threat Clock (external deadline pressure)

Impose an external deadline on each building phase. Without time pressure, kingdom-building becomes a leisurely hobby rather than a dramatic engine. The approaching army, the next plague season, the emperor's audit — the clock forces suboptimal choices that generate conflict.

## The Dual-Knowledge Problem

The critical principle is **bidirectionality**: the protagonist must learn FROM the historical system as much as she teaches TO it. Without this, the story collapses into colonial-savior narrative.

### Bidirectional exchange

Historical characters possess _different_ knowledge, not _less_ intelligence. Song physicians accumulated centuries of empirical observations aligning with modern epidemiology — their theoretical framework (qi, wind-cold, damp-heat) differs, but their observations are often accurate. The protagonist's advantage is conceptual synthesis, not superior observation.

### The synthesis scene

The most effective dual-knowledge scenes follow four beats:

1. Protagonist identifies a problem using a modern framework
2. A historical character provides local knowledge, materials, or connections the protagonist lacks
3. Together they create something neither could alone
4. The result has unforeseen consequences neither anticipated

This structure respects both systems, generates unpredictable outcomes, and prevents one-way teaching.

### When the historical system is actually better

Write scenes where the historical approach outperforms the modern one. The protagonist's framework has blind spots; local practitioners have refined techniques over centuries that work for reasons the protagonist does not initially understand.

See `references/dual-knowledge-systems.md` for the Claire Technique, Xin Song's empiricist incrementalism, writing Song dynasty medicine as genuinely sophisticated, and extended synthesis-scene guidance.

## Orientation Pacing

The orientation period correlates strongly with literary ambition.

**Near-zero orientation** (protagonist adapts immediately) — serial-native, avoid in literary fiction.

**Compressed orientation** (1-3 chapters) — works only when backstory justifies extraordinary adaptability. The adaptability itself must be characterization.

**Extended orientation** (entire first arc) — the literary choice. The protagonist's struggle to become functional IS the first book or its first half. Language, social rules, medical systems, political landscape — learning these is compelling narrative, not material to rush through.

### The language gap

Use gradual learning or scholarly approximation, not magical fluency. A modern Mandarin speaker in Song China has "close but not quite" — functional enough to survive, different enough to mark her as foreign. The gap itself becomes a plot element.

### Avoiding museum prose

- **Sensory anchoring**: deliver world details through immediate physical experience, not abstract description
- **Misunderstanding-as-exposition**: the protagonist gets something wrong; the correction teaches protagonist and reader
- **Need-to-know pacing**: reveal information only when the protagonist needs it for an immediate goal
- **Deep POV immersion**: worldbuilding becomes characterization through the protagonist's emotional filter

## Arrival Mechanism

The mechanism matters far less than what it implies about return. The more literary the work, the less explanation the transport receives.

**Key structural choice**: is return possible?

- **No return** — protagonist commits fully; story is about building a new life
- **Return possible** — sustained dual-loyalty tension
- **Return uncertain** — the question itself becomes thematic

For literary fiction, minimize mechanism explanation. Establish permanence early to free the narrative from "get home" quest plotting.

## Teach/Learn Ratio

The ratio of "protagonist teaches" to "protagonist learns" strongly predicts literary quality:

| Work               | Teaches | Learns | Literary quality |
| ------------------ | ------- | ------ | ---------------- |
| Kindred            | 5%      | 95%    | Highest          |
| Xin Song           | 30%     | 70%    | High             |
| Outlander          | 40%     | 60%    | High             |
| Release That Witch | 70%     | 30%    | Medium           |

**Target 35-40 teaches / 60-65 learns for literary fiction** that preserves kingdom-building satisfaction. The protagonist should spend roughly twice as much narrative time learning from the historical world as teaching to it.

## Failure Modes

Ten recurring failure modes destroy transmigration fiction. Audit every chapter against this list.

| Failure mode                 | One-line diagnostic                                                                        |
| ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Competence lectures          | Narrative becomes Wikipedia; deploy knowledge as currency, not explanation                 |
| Genius surrounded by idiots  | Historical characters are stupid so MC looks smart; give them _different_ knowledge        |
| Power fantasy without limits | No genuine tension; narrow the expertise, fail realistically                               |
| Anachronistic value adoption | Historical characters accept modern values after one speech; show real cultural resistance |
| Museum prose                 | Setting reads like textbook; use sensory anchoring and deep POV                            |
| Butterfly effect ignorance   | Changes don't cascade; every fix must create two new problems                              |
| NPC treatment                | Historical people treated as manipulable game characters; force moral reckoning            |
| No grief for lost world      | Protagonist shows no loss reaction; write extended mourning and recurring loneliness       |
| Repetitive face-slapping     | Endless underestimate-prove-shock cycles; vary conflicts, let MC lose                      |
| The stolen body problem      | Soul displacement without moral reckoning; address the displacement directly               |
