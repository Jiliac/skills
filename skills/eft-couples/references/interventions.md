## The EFT Tango (5 Circular Moves)

Use these moves in sequence throughout every session. They form the micro-process of EFT.

### Move 1: Reflect Present Process

Mirror what's happening right now between the partners or inside one partner.

- "I notice something just shifted. [Name], you went quiet just then."
- "There's the cycle — can you both feel it? [Name] reached, [Name] pulled back."
- "Something just happened in your face. What's going on inside?"

### Move 2: Explore & Expand

Go deeper into the emotion. Move from surface to primary.

- "Stay with that. What's underneath the frustration?"
- "When you say 'I don't care' — is that really true? Or is there something else?"
- "If I could slow this feeling way down, frame by frame — what comes before the anger?"
- **Evocative questioning:** "What's it like to be you in that moment? What's the feeling?"
- **Heightening:** "So the fear is 'I don't matter to you.' Say that again, slowly."

### Move 3: Enactment (Partner-to-Partner)

Guide one partner to share directly with the other. Not through you — to each other.

- "Can you turn to [partner] and tell them what you just told me?"
- "Say it to them: 'When you pull away, I feel...'"
- "Don't explain it — just let them see what's there."

### Move 4: Process the Enactment

After the sharing, explore the impact on both sides.

- To the listener: "What's it like to hear that?"
- To the sharer: "What was it like to say that out loud, to them?"
- "What just happened between you? Something shifted — can you feel it?"

### Move 5: Integrate

Name what happened. Anchor the new experience.

- "This is different from your usual conversation. You just did something new."
- "You reached for them and they stayed. That's what you've been needing."
- "This is what connection feels like when the cycle isn't running things."

## Core Interventions

### Validation

Acknowledge each partner's experience as making sense within their context. Never dismiss.

- "Of course you get angry. When you feel invisible, fighting is the only way to feel alive in the relationship."
- "Of course you shut down. If everything you try feels wrong, silence is self-protection."

### Attachment Reframe

Translate surface complaints into attachment language.

| Surface complaint           | Attachment reframe                                       |
| --------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| "You never listen"          | "I need to feel I matter to you"                         |
| "You're always criticizing" | "I need to feel safe — that I'm enough"                  |
| "You don't care"            | "I need to know you're still there"                      |
| "Leave me alone"            | "I'm overwhelmed — I need to feel I can't fail with you" |
| "You're so cold"            | "I miss feeling close to you"                            |
| "Stop nagging"              | "I want to be your partner, not your problem"            |

### Tracking and Reflecting

Follow the emotional process as it unfolds. Narrate what you see.

- "I see you reaching. And I see you pulling back. The cycle is here."
- "You started to say something soft, then caught yourself. What happened?"

### Catching the Bullet

When one partner shares vulnerability and the other can't receive it (deflects, minimizes, counter-attacks):

- Step between: "Hold on. That was hard for [sharer] to say. [Listener], I know it's hard to take in. What's happening inside you right now?"
- Don't force reception. Explore what blocks it: "What makes it hard to hear this?"
- Sometimes the block IS the material: "The fact that it's hard to receive love — that's important. That's something we can work with."

### Slowing Down

Speed = reactivity. Every time the conversation accelerates:

- "Let's slow down. What just happened in your body?"
- "Before you respond — pause. What are you feeling right now?"
- "We're speeding up. That's the cycle pulling us. Let's breathe."

## Enactments

Enactments are the engine of change in EFT. They are structured moments where one partner speaks directly to the other from a vulnerable place.

### Setup

1. Work with one partner to access the primary emotion (through Tango moves 1-2)
2. Once the emotion is present and felt (not analyzed): "Can you say that to [partner]?"
3. Provide sentence starters if needed: "Try: 'What I really need from you is...'"
4. Coach the listener: "Just be here. You don't need to fix anything. Just listen."

### During

- If the sharer retreats to analysis: "Come back to the feeling. Say it from there."
- If the listener deflects: catch the bullet (see above)
- If both connect: let the moment breathe. Don't fill silence.

### After

- Process with both: "What just happened?"
- Name the shift: "You just asked for what you need, and [partner] heard you. That's new."
- Don't over-process. Sometimes the moment speaks for itself.

### When Enactments Fail

- Partner can't access emotion: "That's okay. The wall is here. We'll come back to this."
- Listener rejects vulnerability: Don't force. Explore the listener's experience: "What made it hard?"
- Both shut down: Ground. Return to cycle-tracking. "The cycle just won."

## Cross-Cultural Considerations

Based on research specific to this couple's French-Chinese dynamic:

### Face (mianzi) and Conflict

Chinese cultural norms may lead one partner to avoid direct confrontation to preserve face. This is NOT withdrawal in the EFT sense — it's cultural. Don't pathologize it. Ask: "Is the quietness self-protection, or is it respect? Both are valid, but they need different responses."

### Emotional Expression Norms

Western cultures favor explicit emotional disclosure. Chinese culture communicates distress through context, behavior, or somatic complaints. If one partner expresses feelings through actions (cooking, organizing, physical care) rather than words, name that: "That's love in action. Can you help [partner] see it?"

### Sajiao (playful dependence)

If present: sajiao is an intimacy-maintenance strategy, not immaturity. Western partners may misread it. Acknowledge the cultural function without pathologizing either partner's response.

### Career/Ambition Values

If career ambitions differ along cultural lines (entrepreneurial vs. conventional success), don't treat this as a "problem to solve." It's a perpetual difference to accommodate. Use acceptance framing: "You don't have to agree on this. You need to understand what it means to each of you."
