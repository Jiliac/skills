# The Negative Interaction Cycle

The cycle is the central concept in EFT. It's the enemy — not either partner.

## What It Is

A repeating pattern where each partner's protective behavior triggers the other's protective behavior. It's self-reinforcing and escalating.

## The Most Common Pattern: Pursue-Withdraw

```
Pursuer feels disconnected
    → reaches out (criticism, questions, demands, emotion)
        → Withdrawer feels overwhelmed/inadequate
            → withdraws (silence, deflection, leaving, "I don't know")
                → Pursuer feels more disconnected
                    → pursues harder (louder, more intense, escalates)
                        → Withdrawer withdraws deeper
                            → [cycle repeats, intensifies]
```

## Other Patterns

**Pursue-Pursue:** Both partners escalate (loud conflict, mutual criticism). Less common. Usually one partner is a "secondary pursuer" — they pursue in response to feeling criticized.

**Withdraw-Withdraw:** Both partners shut down. The relationship goes cold. Often a late-stage pattern after years of pursue-withdraw — the pursuer gave up.

## Surface Behaviors vs. Primary Emotions

| Partner    | Surface behavior              | Looks like                  | Primary emotion underneath            |
| ---------- | ----------------------------- | --------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| Pursuer    | Criticism, demands, questions | Anger, frustration, control | Loneliness, fear of abandonment, hurt |
| Withdrawer | Silence, deflection, leaving  | Indifference, coldness      | Shame, overwhelm, fear of failure     |

**The key insight:** Both partners are responding to the same core fear — disconnection. They just cope with it differently.

## How to Map It With a Couple

### Step 1: Pick a recent small conflict

Not the biggest fight. Something everyday: who does dishes, a misunderstood text, a plan that fell through.

### Step 2: Slow-motion replay

Walk through it second by second with each partner:

- "What happened first?"
- "What did you feel?"
- "What did you do?"
- "Then what did your partner do?"
- "What did you feel then?"
- "What did you do next?"

### Step 3: Draw the circle

Put it on paper (or describe it verbally):

- [Partner A behavior] → [Partner B feeling] → [Partner B behavior] → [Partner A feeling] → back to start

### Step 4: Name it together

Ask the couple to give their cycle a name or metaphor:

- "The spiral"
- "The freeze"
- "The wall and the storm"
- "The chase"

The name externalizes the pattern. It becomes something they can spot together: "We're doing the thing again."

### Step 5: Add the deeper layer

Under each surface behavior, add the primary emotion:

- "When I [criticize], what I'm really feeling is [alone/scared/hurt]"
- "When I [shut down], what I'm really feeling is [overwhelmed/not enough/afraid]"

## Teaching the Couple About Their Cycle

Key messages to convey:

1. **"The cycle is the problem, not either of you."** You're both caught in it. You're both reacting to pain, not causing it on purpose.

2. **"You're both doing the best you can."** Pursuing is reaching for connection. Withdrawing is protecting yourself from pain. Neither is wrong — but together they create the spiral.

3. **"The cycle has been running your relationship."** Once you can see it, you can start to catch it. Catching it is the first step to changing it.

4. **"You don't need to stop having conflict."** You need to stop letting the cycle hijack the conversation. Conflict with safety is healthy. Conflict inside the cycle is destructive.

## Cycle-Spotting Practice (Between Sessions)

Assign for the week:

- "Watch for the cycle. When it starts, just name it: 'I think we're in the cycle.' That's enough. Don't try to fix it yet."
- Log: What triggered it? What did each of you do? What were you actually feeling underneath?
- If possible: "When you spot it, try one new thing: say the feeling underneath instead of the behavior on top."
