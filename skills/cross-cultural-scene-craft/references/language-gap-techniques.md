# Language-Gap Techniques

Six techniques for rendering partial comprehension on the page, ranked by applicability. Plus the glossary question and the specific challenge of temporal-linguistic displacement.

## 1. Interior Translation with Gaps (HIGH confidence)

**Primary technique.** The protagonist mentally translates what she hears, but the translation has visible holes.

Do NOT write: "He said something she couldn't understand" (telling, not showing).

DO write: Render her attempt to make sense — fragments of understood words, uncertain paraphrases, reliance on gesture and context.

**Example pattern:** "The merchant said something about [price] and [silk], though the middle part — the verb, maybe? — was completely opaque."

**Principle (Juliette Wade):** Focus on what the character CAN perceive — tone, facial expression, isolated recognizable words — rather than treating incomprehension as a blank.

**Directly applicable.** This is the workhorse technique for every scene involving partial comprehension.

## 2. Reaction-Based Register Signaling (HIGH confidence)

Solves the "wrong dialect" problem without requiring the reader to hear the actual difference. Instead of transcribing Song-era Chinese in English (impossible), show characters REACTING to the protagonist's modern speech — confusion, condescension, amusement, wariness.

**Example pattern:** "A character addressed her and she responded; the woman's face shifted to something between puzzlement and pity, as though she were speaking to a foreigner — or a simpleton."

**Key advantage of temporal displacement:** The mismatch IS the story. Unlike standard historical fiction (where the author fakes an old-sounding voice), here the protagonist's modern speech is itself a source of dramatic tension.

**Directly applicable.** Use whenever the protagonist speaks and her modern register clashes with period expectations.

## 3. Calque / Literal Translation (HIGH confidence)

Render Song-era idioms and expressions in deliberately literal English. The slightly "off" quality signals temporal-linguistic distance without incomprehensibility.

**Model (Chabon, The Yiddish Policemen's Union):** Yiddish idioms rendered in literal English ("Go shit in the ocean") retain cultural logic while being fully readable.

**Application:** Classical Chinese four-character expressions (chengyu) and Song-era metaphors rendered literally create productive strangeness — the reader feels the cultural distance through language texture.

**Historically grounded.** Proven in Chabon, Rushdie. Classical Chinese idioms are ideal material.

## 4. Contextual Inference (MEDIUM confidence — use sparingly)

Neither character nor reader is given a translation; both must infer meaning from action and outcome.

**Model (McCarthy, The Crossing):** Untranslated Spanish works because surrounding narrative action ensures readers follow the plot even when specific dialogue is opaque.

**Caution — empirical evidence:** A Cambridge fMRI study found that foreign-language text produces less neural synchronization between semantic and emotional brain areas. A study of 1,241 participants found that code-mixing in narratives "felt more difficult to process and, in turn, led to more negative out-group bias and less narrative engagement."

**Use for:** Moments of total incomprehension only. Not a default.

## 5. Scattered Historical Words with Natural Explanation (HIGH confidence)

Drop individual period terms into modern prose with the translation woven into the sentence. Cornwell does this with Anglo-Saxon terms in _The Last Kingdom_; for Song China, the same pattern applies.

**Example:** _She was told to present herself at the yamen — the local magistrate's office, she gathered._

**Cisneros sandwich technique:** Spanish word embedded in a sentence that naturally explains it: "'Que bueno, mi'ha, that's good.'"

**Provides:** Period specificity without comprehension barriers. Good for culturally loaded terms that need to stay in the original.

## 6. Emotional Code-Switching (MEDIUM confidence)

The protagonist reverts to modern French or modern Mandarin at moments of stress — curses, endearments, prayers — producing expressions that sound bizarre to Song-era listeners.

**Principle (Ocean Vuong):** Bilingual speakers switch languages at moments of emotional intensity.

**Application:** Moments of stress produce modern expressions or French words, creating contrast with attempts at period-appropriate speech. The code-switch reveals the protagonist's real cultural identity when performance breaks down.

**Adaptation required.** Well-documented in bilingual fiction but less tested for temporal (same-language) displacement.

## The Glossary Question

**Glossaries break immersion for fiction.** Footnotes are a genre-specific device (R.F. Kuang uses them in Babel as a deliberate academic-subversive gesture, not as a reader aid).

**The protagonist IS the glossary.** Her partial translations and uncertain interpretations serve as the reader's guide. If the protagonist doesn't understand a term, the reader doesn't need to either. If the protagonist does understand, the translation happens in her head, on the page.

## The Three-New-Things Rule

Maximum three unfamiliar terms or concepts per scene. See `pacing-death-traps.md` for the full analysis and review data on foreign term overload.

## Temporal-Linguistic Displacement: The Specific Challenge

A modern Mandarin speaker in Song dynasty China faces a situation unlike anything in published reference works:

- **Shogun:** Total language barrier
- **Outlander:** No real language barrier
- **Kindred:** Shared language weaponized by power

The Song scenario has a PARTIAL barrier within the same language family across 950 years of evolution. She shares a writing system and many root morphemes, but Middle Chinese pronunciation differed dramatically (entering tones, different vowel systems, initial consonant clusters). Grammar, vocabulary, and social registers are centuries apart.

She is not hearing a foreign language — she is hearing a wrong version of her own.

**Solution:** Combine techniques 1 (interior translation with gaps), 2 (reaction-based register signaling), and 3 (calque) in a new configuration. The protagonist partially understands, partially guesses, and is constantly marked as "off" by her modern speech patterns.
