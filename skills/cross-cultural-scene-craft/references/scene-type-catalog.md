# Cross-Cultural Scene Type Catalog

Nine scene types that form the structural vocabulary of cross-cultural fiction. These are not optional set pieces — they are the genre's core dramatic engines.

## 1. Cultural Mistake

**Core tension:** Protagonist violates a rule they didn't know existed.

**5-step structure:**

1. **Setup** — Establish the character's existing behavior (from their native culture)
2. **Violation** — Character acts according to their own norms, unwittingly breaking local rules
3. **Consequence** — Social reaction (shock, offense, danger) creates immediate stakes
4. **Learning** — Through the consequence, both character and reader learn the cultural rule
5. **Adaptation or resistance** — Character adjusts or doubles down, generating further tension

**Key craft insight:** The dramatic interest lies in the social consequence and the character's emotional response to having transgressed — not in the information (the rule itself).

**Powerful inversion (Abercrombie):** Show characters NOT reacting to something the reader finds shocking. A Song dynasty character casually mentioning foot-binding without comment teaches the reader more than a paragraph of explanation.

**Model:** Shogun — Blackthorne fails to bow; a villager is beheaded. The social hierarchy is taught through violence, not exposition.

## 2. Language Negotiation

**Core tension:** Protagonist must communicate across a linguistic gap.

**Craft challenge:** Render partial comprehension without boring the reader or halting narrative momentum.

**Technique:** Show the protagonist's attempt to make sense of what they hear — fragments of understood words, uncertain paraphrases, reliance on gesture and context. Focus on what the character CAN perceive (tone, facial expression, isolated recognizable words) rather than treating incomprehension as a blank.

**Unreliable translator variant:** When the protagonist's only linguistic bridge is biased or hostile, even "understood" information cannot be trusted. This doubles the dramatic tension.

**Model:** Shogun — untranslated Japanese on the page; Father Sebastio translates but is hostile, making even understood dialogue suspect.

## 3. Learning Acquisition

**Core tension:** Protagonist absorbs new cultural or institutional knowledge.

**The "character needs to know" principle:** Embed information in urgent need, never in curiosity. The protagonist learns because their survival, mission, or immediate goal requires it. If the character doesn't need the information right now, the reader doesn't need it either.

**Filter through profession:** An epidemiologist notices sanitation infrastructure, water sources, crowd density, disease indicators. She does NOT notice architectural style or political symbolism — unless those details connect to her survival.

**Model:** Outlander — Claire identifies Colum's Toulouse-Lautrec syndrome during her first medical examination, learning clan politics through her professional lens.

## 4. Teaching Across Cultures

**Core tension:** Protagonist transmits modern knowledge to people who lack the conceptual framework.

**The largest craft gap.** No MFA-level craft text addresses how to write scenes where a character transmits knowledge across a conceptual gap. This scene type has no published pedagogy.

**Core principle:** Make the act of teaching carry its own dramatic stakes. The protagonist doesn't just explain — she must persuade, demonstrate, and risk being thought mad or dangerous. The knowledge itself becomes a source of conflict.

**Avoid:** "As you know, Bob" exposition. The audience for the teaching is in-world characters, not the reader. The drama comes from the characters' reactions — resistance, fear, excitement, suspicion.

**Model:** Kindred — Dana teaches Rufus to read. The act of teaching is itself dangerous (literacy in enslaved people is punishable). The stakes are not pedagogical but existential.

## 5. Interior Translation

**Core tension:** Protagonist mentally converts between two conceptual frameworks.

**Craft challenge:** Managing the reader's cognitive load while signaling which framework is "native" and which is performed.

**Technique:** Use a formal device (confession, journal, internal monologue) that makes the ongoing mental conversion visible. The protagonist's analytical voice becomes the bridge between worlds.

**Model:** The Sympathizer — Nguyen's narrator perpetually translates between Vietnamese and American frames, using confession as the formal device.

## 6. Orientation Through Crisis

**Core tension:** Reader learns the world because the protagonist's survival depends on it.

**Craft challenge:** Maintain urgency while delivering enough detail for comprehension. Crisis must not become confusion.

**Structure:** Physical disorientation first, then sensory contrast, then total barrier (language, custom, law), then violence or consequence as social calibration.

**Risk:** Front-loading action without emotional warmth alienates readers who need a character to care about before tolerating disorientation.

**Model:** Kindred — Dana arrives in antebellum Maryland with zero preparation; every cultural detail is learned under mortal threat.

## 7. The Assumption Trap

**Core tension:** Locals assume the protagonist knows something they don't.

**The real-time choice:** Fake understanding or confess ignorance. Both have costs. Faking risks deeper exposure later. Confessing reveals foreignness immediately.

**For diasporic protagonists:** This is the equivalent of crisis-first orientation — learning the culture under social pressure rather than physical threat.

**Model:** Pachinko — Korean characters in Japan constantly assumed to know Japanese rituals; their accents or errors "out" them as foreign.

## 8. The "Found Out" Moment

**Core tension:** Protagonist's outsider status is revealed through a small, specific error.

**Craft rule:** Use a tiny cultural detail, not dramatic confrontation. The reveal should feel inevitable but devastating. Speech betrays what appearance conceals.

**Technique:** Build toward the moment through accumulated near-misses, so the reader feels the tension before the exposure.

**Model:** Pachinko — Yoseb "could approach any Japanese and receive a polite smile, but he'd lose the welcome as soon as he said anything."

## 9. The Unexpected Connection

**Core tension:** Protagonist discovers a resonance with the unfamiliar culture they didn't expect.

**Craft rule:** Earn the emotional payoff without sentimentality. Make it sensory (smell, gesture, rhythm) rather than intellectual. These moments are more moving BECAUSE of the surrounding failures.

**Technique:** Use involuntary recognition — a phrase that echoes something the protagonist's mother said, a smell that triggers half-buried memory, a gesture made without thinking that turns out to be correct.

**Model:** The Joy Luck Club — Jing-mei in China: "I think, My mother was right. I am becoming Chinese." The recognition is visceral, not analytical.
