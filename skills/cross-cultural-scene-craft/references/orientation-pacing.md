# Orientation Pacing

Three landmark models for orienting readers to unfamiliar worlds, plus the 50-page patience window and recovery techniques.

## Crisis-First Model (Shogun)

**Approach:** Shipwreck and imprisonment. The reader's first experience is survival urgency, not worldbuilding.

**Orientation sequence:**

1. Physical disorientation (waking in an unfamiliar room)
2. Sensory contrast (extreme cleanliness vs. filthy European ships)
3. Total language barrier (untranslated Japanese on the page)
4. Violence as social calibration (a samurai beheads a villager who fails to bow — teaching the entire social hierarchy in one act)

**Action:orientation ratio:** ~70:30 in first 50 pages.

**Key technique:** The protagonist's only translator is hostile and unreliable, so even "understood" information cannot be trusted.

**Risk:** Readers report needing 200-300 pages before it "clicks." DNFs occur as early as page 66. Relentless brutality without emotional warmth alienates readers who need a character to care about before tolerating disorientation.

**Use when:** The world is so alien that gradual immersion would feel arbitrary — the reader needs a reason to push through disorientation.

## Competence-as-Entry Model (Outlander)

**Approach:** Establish the protagonist's modern voice, professional competence, and relationships BEFORE displacement. When displacement occurs, competence is their survival currency.

**Orientation sequence:**

1. ~50 pages establishing character in familiar world
2. Displacement event
3. Within hours, protagonist uses professional skill (Claire sets Jamie's shoulder)
4. Competence earns passage into the local community's protection

**Action:texture:character ratio:** ~40:40:20 in first 50 pages.

**Key technique:** The protagonist's modern voice creates constant orientation dialogue — "smart-ass modern remarks about everything" produce comedic defamiliarization. The reader processes the unfamiliar world through a familiar sensibility.

**Risk:** The slow pre-displacement opening causes DNFs. "This is my third time trying to get through this book" is a representative complaint. Also, no true language barrier exists (Claire speaks English; Gaelic is atmospheric), so the barrier is cultural rather than linguistic.

**Use when:** The protagonist has a skill the new world needs, creating a natural integration path.

## Hostile-Setting Model (Kindred)

**Approach:** The world is actively dangerous to the protagonist's identity, not just unfamiliar. No gradual immersion because the setting is existentially hostile.

**Orientation sequence:**

1. Prologue: catastrophic consequence shown first ("I lost an arm on my last trip home")
2. First displacement: minutes long, maximally violent (drowning rescue, racial slur, gun)
3. Each subsequent trip: longer and more horrific
4. Return mechanism tied to mortal danger (thematic statement as plot device)

**Action:reflection ratio:** ~80:20 in first 50 pages.

**Key technique:** Unlike Blackthorne (curiosity) or Claire (suspicion-but-protection), Dana is in existential danger at every moment by virtue of who she is. The back-and-forth provides respite.

**Risk:** The alternating structure can feel repetitive if escalation is insufficient.

**Use when:** The world is actively dangerous to the protagonist's identity — their existence in it is the conflict.

## Which Model When

| Scenario                                                   | Best Fit                                             | Why                                                                  |
| ---------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| World is utterly alien, no foothold                        | Crisis-first                                         | Reader needs urgency to push through disorientation                  |
| Protagonist has a useful skill                             | Competence-entry                                     | Natural integration path; skill earns trust                          |
| World is hostile to protagonist's identity                 | Hostile-setting                                      | Existence IS the conflict                                            |
| Diasporic protagonist (looks like them, isn't one of them) | Competence-entry + hostile-setting emotional texture | Competence provides structural engine; shame provides emotional core |

For a diasporic protagonist with professional skills: start with competence-entry structure, layer in hostile-setting emotional dynamics as the protagonist discovers how much they don't know about a culture they feel they should know.

## The 50-Page Patience Window

Readers have approximately a 50-page patience window — without an emotional anchor or plot hook by then, DNF rates spike. See `pacing-death-traps.md` for the full analysis with examples from Shogun, Name of the Rose, and Outlander.

## Recovery Techniques After Heavy Worldbuilding

When orientation-heavy sections are necessary, four techniques restore momentum:

1. **Action beat reset** — Follow exposition with immediate physical action or conflict
2. **Emotional grounding** — Return to the character's personal desire or fear after information delivery
3. **Diegetic worldbuilding** — Make cultural detail the action itself (Cherryh's Foreigner sets meetings around tea ceremonies so the cultural detail IS the action)
4. **Scene-first construction** — "You only build what the next scene needs." Instead of inventing an entire administrative structure, write the scene where the protagonist tries to get a travel permit

## Maximum Orientation-to-Action Ratio

~30/70 per chapter in the first quarter of the novel.

In practice: for every paragraph of cultural information, provide at least two paragraphs of action, dialogue, or character-driven forward motion.
