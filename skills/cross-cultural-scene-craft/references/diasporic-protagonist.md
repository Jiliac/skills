# The Diasporic Protagonist

Craft guidance for writing protagonists who look like they belong but don't — the specific emotional and structural dynamics of diasporic fish-out-of-water, distinct from white-outsider displacement.

## Why Diasporic Fish-Out-of-Water Is Emotionally Different

The white outsider (Blackthorne, Claire) experiences curiosity, confusion, and danger. The diasporic protagonist experiences **shame** — the specific, devastating shame of not knowing a culture they feel they should know.

**Canonical scene (Amy Tan, "Fish Cheeks"):** A Chinese-American girl is mortified by her family's Chinese dinner when her white crush visits. The shame is directed at her own people's customs because she sees them through outsider eyes.

**Pachinko (Mozasu visiting Korea):** "In Seoul, people like me get called Japanese bastards, and in Japan, I'm just another dirty Korean." The homeland rejects you too.

The critical craft distinction: the diasporic protagonist cannot be a tourist in their own heritage. John Rieder's "colonial gaze" gives knowledge and power to the outsider who looks, while denying agency to the culture being observed. A diasporic protagonist reverses this — they are the one being looked at, being judged by the culture.

## Comparison Table: White Outsider vs. Diasporic Protagonist

| Dimension         | White Outsider (Blackthorne)                   | Diasporic Protagonist                              |
| ----------------- | ---------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Visibility        | Immediately identified as foreign              | Assumed to be local until they fail                |
| Power dynamic     | Often brings privilege or status               | Must earn belonging from a position of deficit     |
| Stakes of failure | Social embarrassment, physical danger          | Existential identity crisis — "I should know this" |
| Narrative role    | Explorer/bridge for Western reader             | Character in crisis, not a tourist                 |
| Reader position   | Discovering the culture alongside the outsider | Witnessing someone fail their "own" heritage       |

## Five Scene Techniques for the Diasporic Dimension

### 1. The Body's Betrayal

Physical gestures carry cultural information the protagonist cannot fully control. The body carries its real culture even when the mind is trying to perform.

**Technique:** A French shrug instead of a Chinese nod. Reaching for utensils differently. An instinct to shake hands that doesn't exist in this world. These are involuntary — the protagonist cannot rehearse them away.

**Model (Celeste Ng):** Renders between-ness through the body — the way characters are categorized by their features, the physical awkwardness of performing a culture that isn't yours.

### 2. The Assumption Trap

Locals assume the protagonist knows things she doesn't — family obligations, seasonal rituals, literary references, political allegiances. Each assumption creates a real-time choice: fake understanding or confess ignorance.

**Technique:** Present the dilemma explicitly in internal monologue. Show the calculation: what does she lose by admitting ignorance? What does she risk by pretending?

**Model (Pachinko):** Korean characters passing as Japanese face this constantly — correcting the error reveals foreignness; playing along risks deeper exposure later.

### 3. The Small Cultural Error

Micro-failures that accumulate into a pattern revealing the protagonist as an imposter. Not a single dramatic unmasking but a slow erosion of performed identity.

**Technique:** Wrong color worn to a ceremony. Serving herself before elders. Using a familiar form of address with someone of higher station. Each error is small; the pattern is damning.

**Model (Charles Yu, Interior Chinatown):** Literalizes identity as performance — characters assigned roles like "Generic Asian Man" must level up. The same logic applies: the protagonist must perform "Song dynasty woman" convincingly.

### 4. The "Almost" Moment

Flashes of unexpected belonging — a phrase echoing something her mother said, a smell triggering half-buried memory, a gesture made without thinking that turns out correct.

**Technique:** Place these after sustained failure sequences. The involuntary recognition — sensory, not intellectual — is more moving BECAUSE of the surrounding failures.

**Model (Amy Tan, Joy Luck Club):** Jing-mei in China: "I think, My mother was right. I am becoming Chinese." The recognition is visceral, not analytical.

### 5. The Confession Voice

A running internal monologue analyzing the protagonist's own performance — sardonic, self-aware, occasionally surprised by her own reactions.

**Technique:** This voice should be analytically trained (French-educated, scientifically rigorous) and emotionally caught between worlds. The self-analysis IS the character voice.

**Model (Nguyen, The Sympathizer):** "I've learned to embrace the fact that my authenticity is my inauthenticity." Perpetual self-analysis as narrative mode.

## The Kishotenketsu Alternative

Matthew Salesses (Craft in the Real World, 2021) argues that Western fiction's conflict-climax-resolution structure is culturally specific. Chinese narrative tradition uses kishotenketsu:

1. **Ki (introduction)** — Establish the situation
2. **Sho (development)** — Develop the situation
3. **Ten (twist)** — A surprise that reframes understanding (NOT confrontation — surprise)
4. **Ketsu (reconciliation)** — Resolution through the new understanding

**Structural opportunity:** The narrative follows Western conflict structure for the protagonist's French-trained analytical mind, while the Song dynasty world around her operates on kishotenketsu logic. Part of her learning is recognizing that the world isn't building toward the confrontation she expects.

## The "Character Needs to Know" Principle

Filter ALL worldbuilding through the protagonist's profession, class, and immediate needs.

An epidemiologist walking into a Song dynasty city notices: sanitation infrastructure, water sources, crowd density, disease indicators, food handling practices.

She does NOT notice: architectural style, political symbolism — unless those details connect to her survival.

Patrick Rothfuss: "If you don't know the culture your character comes from, how can you know what he's really like?" The protagonist's professional lens becomes the reader's lens for understanding Song China — specific, practical, urgently filtered.

## Worldbuilding Through Action vs. Exposition

Le Guin (Steering the Craft): "Crafty writers don't allow Exposition to form Lumps. They break up the information, grind it fine, and make it into bricks to build the story with."

Sanderson's extension: "Expand what you have before you add something new." Fewer, deeper cultural elements beat many shallow ones.

**The newcomer POV is the single most powerful worldbuilding device.** When the protagonist genuinely needs to learn the world, every explanation feels organic. The character's confusion is the reader's confusion.

**The cultural mistake scene is the most efficient scene type** — it simultaneously delivers worldbuilding AND dramatic tension. Build around them.
