# Cross-Cultural Scene Craft

Draft scenes where a protagonist navigates an unfamiliar culture — cultural mistakes, language barriers, learning sequences, teaching across conceptual gaps. This skill covers the structural vocabulary of cross-cultural fiction: scene types, orientation pacing, language-gap rendering, and diasporic identity dynamics.

## When to Use

- Draft scenes involving cultural displacement, fish-out-of-water dynamics, or diasporic return
- Write language-gap dialogue (partial comprehension, temporal-linguistic displacement)
- Structure orientation pacing for an unfamiliar world
- Write teaching scenes where knowledge must cross a conceptual gap
- Handle diasporic protagonist identity (shame, belonging, performance failure)

## How This Differs from historical-scene-drafting

`historical-scene-drafting` is for Harris-style "men in rooms" political scenes — dialogue combat, power dynamics, subtext beneath courteous phrasing. It assumes the protagonist belongs to the world.

This skill is for scenes where the protagonist does NOT belong. The dramatic engine is cultural displacement, not political maneuvering. Use both skills together when a displaced protagonist enters a political negotiation.

## Core Principle

**Orientation is an act of survival, not exposition.** The protagonist needs to understand the world to stay alive, and the reader rides that urgency. Worldbuilding appears on the page only when the character needs it to survive, decide, or act in the current scene.

## 9 Cross-Cultural Scene Types

| Scene Type                 | Core Tension                                    | Key Craft Rule                                                    |
| -------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Cultural mistake           | Violates a rule they didn't know existed        | Drama lives in the _consequence_, not the rule                    |
| Language negotiation       | Must communicate across a linguistic gap        | Render the _attempt to understand_, not blank incomprehension     |
| Learning acquisition       | Absorbs new cultural knowledge                  | Embed in urgent need, not curiosity                               |
| Teaching across cultures   | Transmits knowledge across a conceptual gap     | Make the act of teaching carry its own dramatic stakes            |
| Interior translation       | Mentally converts between frameworks            | Signal which framework is native, which is performed              |
| Orientation through crisis | Learns the world because survival depends on it | Maintain urgency while delivering enough detail for comprehension |
| The assumption trap        | Locals assume they know something they don't    | Force a real-time choice: fake understanding or confess ignorance |
| The "found out" moment     | Outsider status revealed through a small error  | Use a tiny cultural detail, not dramatic confrontation            |
| The unexpected connection  | Discovers resonance with the unfamiliar culture | Make it sensory (smell, gesture, rhythm), not intellectual        |

See `references/scene-type-catalog.md` for full craft guidance on each type.

## 3 Orientation Models

| Model                        | Approach                                              | Action:Orientation Ratio            | Risk                                          |
| ---------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| Crisis-first (Shogun)        | Shipwreck/imprisonment — learn or die                 | 70:30                               | Readers need 200-300 pages before it "clicks" |
| Competence-entry (Outlander) | Protagonist's skill buys access                       | 40:40:20 (action:texture:character) | Slow pre-displacement opening causes DNFs     |
| Hostile-setting (Kindred)    | World is actively dangerous to protagonist's identity | 80:20                               | Back-and-forth can feel repetitive            |

Choose based on the protagonist's relationship to the world. See `references/orientation-pacing.md` for detailed guidance.

## 6 Language-Gap Techniques (Ranked)

1. **Interior translation with gaps** (HIGH) — Render the attempt to make sense: fragments, uncertain paraphrases, reliance on gesture
2. **Reaction-based register signaling** (HIGH) — Show characters reacting to the protagonist's "wrong" speech
3. **Calque / literal translation** (HIGH) — Render period idioms in deliberately literal English
4. **Contextual inference** (MEDIUM, use sparingly) — Neither character nor reader gets a translation; both infer from action
5. **Scattered historical words with natural explanation** (HIGH) — "the _yamen_ — the local magistrate's office, she gathered"
6. **Emotional code-switching** (MEDIUM) — Protagonist reverts to native language at moments of stress

The protagonist IS the glossary. No actual glossaries or footnotes. See `references/language-gap-techniques.md` for full guidance.

## The Diasporic Protagonist

When the protagonist looks like they belong but doesn't — the emotional register shifts from curiosity to **shame**. The diasporic protagonist cannot be a tourist in their own heritage.

**5 key dimensions that differ from white-outsider fish-out-of-water:**

| Dimension       | White Outsider                        | Diasporic Protagonist            |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Visibility      | Immediately identified as foreign     | Assumed local until they fail    |
| Power           | Often brings privilege or status      | Must earn belonging from deficit |
| Stakes          | Social embarrassment, physical danger | Existential identity crisis      |
| Narrative role  | Explorer/bridge for reader            | Character in crisis              |
| Reader position | Discovering alongside outsider        | Witnessing heritage failure      |

**5 scene techniques:** body's betrayal, assumption trap, small cultural error, "almost" moment, confession voice. See `references/diasporic-protagonist.md`.

## Worldbuilding Through Action

- The newcomer POV is the single most powerful worldbuilding device — their confusion IS the reader's confusion
- Cultural mistake scenes are the most efficient: they deliver worldbuilding AND dramatic tension simultaneously
- Filter all worldbuilding through the protagonist's profession, class, and immediate needs
- **If the information does not change a choice in the next page, cut it or delay it**
- Maximum 3 unfamiliar terms or concepts per scene

## Pacing Death Traps

1. **The 50-page patience window** — No emotional anchor by page 50 = DNF spike
2. **The three-new-things rule** — Max 3 unfamiliar terms per scene or readers start skimming
3. **The tea ceremony problem** — Beautiful cultural detail that stops the plot is homework, not fiction
4. **The Shogun ending problem** — Orientation creates an implicit promise of payoff; deliver it
