# Anachronism Types and Detection Techniques

## The Three Categories of Anachronism

A single glaring anachronism can "prick the bubble of belief," destroying reader trust. Professional historical fiction writers audit their manuscripts across three distinct categories, each requiring different detection methods.

## 1. Factual Anachronisms

**Definition:** Physical objects, technologies, or verifiable events placed in the wrong chronological time.

### Examples

**Technology/Inventions:**

- Stirrups in early medieval Europe (introduced around 8th century, not earlier)
- Hard-boiled detective walking across Golden Gate Bridge in 1929 (opened 1937)
- Character using $500 bill after 1969 (discontinued)
- Mechanical typewriters in 1860s (commercially available 1870s)
- Zippers in Victorian novels (patented 1893, popular 1920s+)

**Food/Goods:**

- Tomatoes in medieval European cooking (New World crop, post-1500s in Europe)
- Potatoes in Renaissance England (introduced late 16th century)
- Chocolate in ancient Rome (New World)
- Coffee in 14th-century Europe (arrived 17th century)

**Events:**

- Character witnessing event that happened decades earlier/later
- Referencing a law not yet passed
- Discussing a book not yet published

### Detection Techniques

**1. Encyclopedia verification**

- Check invention/patent dates
- Verify when objects entered common use (invention ≠ widespread adoption)
- Cross-reference multiple sources (Wikipedia as starting point, verify with specialized sources)

**2. Visual archives**

- Museum databases (V&A, Smithsonian, British Museum online collections)
- Contemporary paintings and illustrations
- Photograph archives (for 19th-20th century)
- Fashion plates and pattern books

**3. Timeline tools**

- Create parallel timeline: fictional plot vs. real-world events/inventions
- Aeon Timeline or similar software to visualize chronology
- Spreadsheet with character ages, historical events, technology availability

**4. Subject-matter experts**

- Consult specialists for technical details (weapons, medicine, architecture)
- Historical societies and reenactment groups
- Museum curators and archivists

**Confidence level:** **HIGH** - Easiest to prevent with systematic checking.

## 2. Tonal and Linguistic Anachronisms

**Definition:** Vocabulary, idioms, slang, or tonal register that clashes with the era. Characters using language not yet in the lexicon, or adopting speech patterns from a different time period.

### Examples

**Modern slang in period dialogue:**

- "Bog standard" in 1930s (phrase emerged 1960s)
- "Step on it" in 1917 (popularized 1920s+)
- Regency earl calling butler "bozo" (American slang, 1920s)
- Tudor character saying "OK" (American origin, 1839)

**Archaic pastiche (Museum Prose):**

- "Prithee, good sir, wouldst thou brook such perfidy?" (Victorian medieval fantasy, not authentic)
- Excessive "thee/thou" in Shakespeare-adjacent periods (actual usage more complex)
- "Forsooth," "marry," "zounds" overused (rare in actual speech)

**Tonal mismatches:**

- Informal, chatty tone in rigidly formal court setting
- Modern psychological terminology ("I'm having a panic attack," "That's gaslighting")
- Therapy-speak or self-help jargon in pre-20th century
- Business jargon in pre-industrial contexts ("synergy," "leverage," "stakeholders")

**Conceptual vocabulary:**

- "Sadist" before Marquis de Sade (18th century)
- "Scientist" before 1834 (previously "natural philosopher")
- "Homosexual" before 1869 (medical term, not self-identification)
- "Freelance" in medieval context (term emerged 1800s for mercenary knights, popularized 20th century)

### Detection Techniques

**1. Oxford English Dictionary (OED)**

The OED is the historical novelist's primary diagnostic tool.

**How to use:**

- Look up every suspicious word
- Check "Date" section (earliest recorded use)
- Read chronological quotations (shows how usage evolved)
- Note when term became common (not just first coinage)

**Example:** "Scientist" first coined 1834, but "natural philosopher" remained standard until late 19th century. Using "scientist" in 1840s dialogue is technically accurate but tonally wrong.

**2. Online Etymology Dictionary**

- Quick lookup for word origins
- Shows how meanings shifted over time
- Identifies borrowed words from other languages

**3. Linguistic corpora**

- Birmingham University's CLiC for 19th-century language
- Google Ngram Viewer (shows word frequency over time, but requires critical interpretation)
- Period dictionaries (Johnson's 1755 Dictionary for 18th century, etc.)

**4. Read-aloud test**

- If you stumble over dialogue, it's too archaic
- If it sounds like modern speech, check for anachronistic slang
- Period dialogue should feel natural but not contemporary

**5. Narrative vs. Dialogue Rule**

**Standard industry practice:**

- **Narrative prose:** Can use slightly modern descriptive terms to aid reader comprehension
- **Character dialogue:** Must be strictly period-appropriate

**Example:**

**Acceptable (narrative):** "The ambassador's sadistic pleasure in the execution was evident."
**Unacceptable (dialogue):** 16th-century character saying, "He's such a sadist."

**Why:** Readers understand the author is translating for comprehension, but characters must speak authentically.

**Confidence level:** **MEDIUM** - Requires linguistic research and judgment calls.

## 3. Conceptual Anachronisms (Most Insidious)

**Definition:** Assigning modern psychology, 21st-century morality, post-Enlightenment scientific understanding, or contemporary social attitudes to characters from earlier periods.

This is the **hardest to detect** and the **most common failure** in historical fiction.

### Examples

**Modern psychology in historical minds:**

- Medieval character analyzing their own trauma through psychoanalytic lens
- Ancient Roman discussing "self-esteem" or "emotional intelligence"
- Tudor courtier having modern egalitarian views on class without justification
- Viking warrior expressing 21st-century feminist ideology

**Anachronistic scientific knowledge:**

- Early artillerymen understanding Galilean acceleration (concept not formulated)
- Medieval doctor knowing germ theory (pre-microscope, pre-Pasteur)
- Ancient Greek understanding evolution or genetics
- Renaissance character knowing heliocentrism before Copernicus

**Modern moral frameworks:**

- Historical character expressing horror at slavery/colonialism using 21st-century ethical framework
- Medieval character believing in religious tolerance (concept developed post-Enlightenment)
- Ancient character valuing individual autonomy over family/clan obligation
- Historical figure with modern views on gender equality without textual justification

**Contemporary social attitudes:**

- Characters discussing "human rights" before the concept existed
- Medieval character believing in meritocracy over hereditary status
- Ancient character rejecting superstition without period-appropriate intellectual foundation
- Historical figure expressing modern environmentalism

### Why This Matters

The past is **fundamentally strange**. Historical fiction must embrace the **alterity** (otherness) of the era.

- People in the past did not think like modern people
- They held beliefs we consider repugnant
- They had different psychological frameworks, ethical systems, and worldviews

**Sanitizing the past** to appease modern sensibilities destroys authenticity.

### Detection Techniques

**1. Mindset Auditing**

For every scene, ask:

- **Are the character's reactions driven by:**
  - Period's religious terrors, superstitions, rigid social hierarchies? ✓
  - Modern egalitarian values, secular worldview, psychological self-awareness? ✗

- **Does the character's worldview match their time?**
  - Do they fear hell, divine punishment, witchcraft (if period-appropriate)? ✓
  - Do they believe in class hierarchy, gender roles, ethnic superiority (if historically accurate)? ✓
  - Do they express modern therapeutic language or 21st-century social justice concepts? ✗

**2. The "Thought-World" Test**

Read primary sources from the period. Immerse in the **actual psychology** of the era.

**Sources to consult:**

- Diaries and letters (reveals private fears and prejudices)
- Sermons and religious texts (shows moral framework)
- Legal codes and court records (reveals what society punished)
- Medical texts (shows how people understood bodies and illness)

**Question:** Would a person who genuinely believed in this period's worldview think/say this?

**3. Justification Requirement for Progressive Characters**

If a character holds unusually progressive views for their time, you MUST provide rigorous justification.

**Example: C.J. Sansom's Matthew Shardlake**

Shardlake is a 16th-century lawyer with mildly skeptical, rationalist views. Sansom justifies this by:

- Shardlake's legal training (exposes him to logical argumentation)
- The period's intellectual currents (birth of rational, skeptical inquiry in Renaissance)
- Specific historical figures who held similar views (e.g., Erasmus, More's early work)
- Showing the social cost (Shardlake is marginalized, endangered by his views)

**Shardlake is NOT a modern liberal in Tudor clothing.** He still:

- Fears God and hell
- Accepts monarchy (questions specific monarchs, not system)
- Operates within class hierarchies
- Holds period-typical prejudices (though questioning some)

**4. Embrace Alterity**

Allow characters to be genuinely strange by modern standards.

**Good:** Medieval character genuinely believing in demons, witchcraft, divine punishment
**Bad:** Medieval character secretly skeptical but hiding it (without historical justification)

**Good:** Roman character viewing slavery as natural order
**Bad:** Roman character expressing abolitionist views (no historical basis)

**Good:** Tudor character terrified of heresy charges, willing to betray friends to save soul
**Bad:** Tudor character with modern commitment to free speech and religious tolerance

**5. Modern Analog Filter vs. Conceptual Accuracy**

There is tension between:

- **Modern Analog Filter** (Skill 4: Thriller Revision) - Making politics feel urgent and modern
- **Conceptual Accuracy** (This skill) - Maintaining period-appropriate psychology

**Resolution:** Use Modern Analog Filter for **plot mechanics** (bribery = lobbying, patronage = VC funding), but maintain **period-authentic psychology** in character mindsets.

**Example:**

**Modern Analog (plot):** Describe a Roman Senate power struggle using modern political urgency
**Conceptual Accuracy (character):** Roman senator's motives driven by honor, family legacy, patron-client obligations (not modern careerism)

**Confidence level:** **LOW** - Hardest to detect, requires deep psychological awareness and constant vigilance.

## The "Tiffany Problem"

**Definition:** A detail that is historically accurate but _feels_ anachronistic to modern readers because it conflicts with popular perception.

**Origin:** The name "Tiffany" dates to the 12th century (from Theophania, Epiphany), but a medieval character named Tiffany will read as a glaring error to modern audiences who associate it with 1980s America.

### Other Examples

- Vibrant colors in medieval clothing (modern assumption: everything was brown/gray)
- Ancient Romans eating lying down (feels like orientalist fantasy, but accurate)
- Victorian use of "cool" as slang (attested, but feels modern)
- Medieval people bathing regularly (contradicts modern myth of medieval filth)

### How to Handle

**Option 1: Substitute**
If the detail breaks immersion and serves no vital purpose, replace it.

- Medieval Tiffany → Theophania (more recognizable medieval form)
- Use period-appropriate alternative that matches reader expectations

**Option 2: Author's Note**
If the detail is essential, include a brief author's note acknowledging the accuracy.

**Option 3: Contextual Integration**
Seamlessly integrate explanation within the narrative.

**Example:**
"She was named Tiffany, after the Epiphany — Theophania in Greek — the day of her birth."

**Caveat:** Only do this if the explanation doesn't halt narrative momentum.

## Summary Table: Anachronism Detection

| Type                 | Definition                                               | Detection Method                                                                  | Difficulty |
| -------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------- |
| **Factual**          | Objects, tech, events in wrong time                      | Encyclopedia checks, visual archives, timeline tools                              | **Easy**   |
| **Tonal/Linguistic** | Wrong vocabulary, idioms, speech patterns                | OED, etymology databases, read-aloud test, narrative vs. dialogue rule            | **Medium** |
| **Conceptual**       | Modern psychology, morality, science in historical minds | Mindset auditing, thought-world test, justification requirement, embrace alterity | **Hard**   |

## Practical Workflow: Three-Pass Anachronism Audit

**Pass 1: Factual Sweep**

- Check all objects, inventions, foods, events against historical timeline
- Verify dates of patents, introductions, widespread adoption
- Cross-reference visual sources (paintings, museum databases)

**Pass 2: Linguistic Sweep**

- Run dialogue through OED for suspicious words
- Apply Narrative vs. Dialogue Rule (flag modern terms in speech)
- Read-aloud test for archaic pastiche or modern slang

**Pass 3: Conceptual Sweep**

- Mindset audit: Are characters thinking with period-appropriate psychology?
- Flag modern moral frameworks, scientific knowledge, social attitudes
- Justify any progressive views with historical intellectual currents
- Ensure characters fear/believe what people of their time actually feared/believed

**Final check:** Ask beta readers or sensitivity readers from the period's academic specialty to flag remaining anachronisms.
