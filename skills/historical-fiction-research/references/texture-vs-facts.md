# Texture vs. Facts: What Novelists Need vs. What Academics Need

## The Fundamental Divide

Historical novelists and academic historians use the same archival sources but extract entirely different data.

### What Academic Historians Need

- Macro trends (economic shifts, demographic changes, geopolitical forces)
- Exhaustive verification (peer-reviewed sources, multiple corroborations)
- Objective truth (what actually happened, supported by evidence)
- Completeness (every relevant factor documented)

**When to stop:** When the archival record goes silent

### What Historical Novelists Need

- Micro reality (immediate, sensory experience of individuals)
- Lived experience (how cold cobbles feel, how power smells, what fear tastes like)
- Psychological truth (emotional resonance within historical context)
- Selectivity (only details that advance story or deepen character)

**When to stop:** When you can hear characters' voices and feel their world

## Texture Defined

**Usable texture** = Historical details that:

- Advance the story
- Deepen characterization
- Root the reader in physical/emotional moment
- Reveal power dynamics or social hierarchies
- Create visceral sensory experience

**Inert facts** = Historical data that:

- Are academically true but narratively inert
- Explain political structures characters wouldn't consciously think about
- Provide context the reader doesn't need for emotional engagement
- Satisfy writer's research obsession but not reader's story hunger

## Examples: Texture vs. Facts

### Roman Senate Scene

**Academic facts (inert for fiction):**

- Senate composition: 300-600 members during different periods
- Voting procedures: Division, hand count, voice vote
- Architectural specifications: Dimensions of Curia Julia
- Constitutional role: Senatus consultum vs. plebiscite

**Novelist texture (usable):**

- Temperature: Stifling heat trapped under marble, sweat stains on wool togas
- Sound: Echo of voices in vaulted space, shuffling sandals, murmured threats
- Power dynamics: Who sits nearest the consul, who gets interrupted, who controls silence
- Physical sensation: Weight of toga restricting movement, vulnerability of bare legs
- Smell: Incense from morning sacrifice, olive oil in hair, wine on breath

### Tudor Court Scene

**Academic facts (inert for fiction):**

- Court structure: Lord Chamberlain's office, Privy Council composition
- Legal framework: Act of Supremacy, Treason Act provisions
- Genealogy: Family trees of major courtiers
- Trade agreements: Wool export quotas to Low Countries

**Novelist texture (usable):**

- Visual: Candlelight making shadows dance on tapestries, faces half-visible
- Tactile: Velvet sleeves catching on rough wood benches, cold air from unglazed windows
- Auditory: Whispers echoing in stone corridors, footsteps revealing who approaches
- Power: Who stands vs. sits, who receives eye contact from king, who gets named first
- Danger: Coded language (talking about grain while meaning treason), what silence means

## Robert Harris's Rule: 10:1 Ratio

Harris gathers **10 times more information than he will use**.

Why?

- Research creates subconscious fluency in the period
- Deep immersion allows texture to surface organically during drafting
- Conscious mind forgets facts; subconscious retains psychological reality

The research is not wasted. It creates the foundation for authentic period voice, but **the writer must actively forget** the academic data during drafting.

## The "Boys in the Basement" Technique

After intensive research, push data to subconscious:

1. **Immersion phase** - Read primary sources intensively, take sensory notes
2. **Incubation phase** - Stop researching, let conscious mind release the data
3. **Drafting phase** - Trust texture to surface organically through character action

**Do not:**

- Keep research files open while drafting
- Consult sources mid-scene to verify facts
- Let academic knowledge override narrative momentum

**Do:**

- Insert placeholders for missing details (e.g., "[CHECK: What did Roman bridles look like?]")
- Maintain prose flow above factual precision
- Fill gaps during revision, not drafting

## Hilary Mantel's "Cinematic" Method

Mantel roots readers in the **narrowed field of vision** of the viewpoint character.

**Not:** Wide, objective historical overview (academic approach)
**Instead:** Character's immediate physical reality (novelist approach)

### Example from Wolf Hall

**Academic approach:**
"In 1529, Cardinal Wolsey fell from power due to his failure to secure Henry VIII's divorce from Catherine of Aragon, triggering a constitutional crisis."

**Mantel's texture:**
"Thomas Cromwell is lying on the cobbles, his cheek pressed to cold stone. He can taste blood and beer. His father's boot is visible at the edge of his vision."

The fall of Wolsey is felt through Cromwell's body, not explained through historical summary.

## The Necessity Test for Info-Dumps

Before including historical exposition, ask:

**Does the story function if this paragraph is removed?**

- If **YES** → The detail is inert. Delete it.
- If **NO** → The detail is weight-bearing. Keep it, but embed in action/dialogue.

### Info-Dump Example (Bad)

"The Senate of Rome was the supreme council of the Roman Republic. It consisted of 300 members, all former magistrates. The Senate controlled finance, foreign policy, and military command, though it technically could only advise the magistrates."

**Why it fails:** Lecture voice, no character filter, stops narrative momentum.

### Embedded Texture (Good)

"Tiro leaned close. 'The Senate can't actually command you to do anything, can they? They just... advise?'

Cicero smiled grimly. 'In theory. But when three hundred former consuls and praetors advise you to fall on your sword, the distinction becomes rather academic.'"

**Why it works:** Info delivered through character dialogue, reveals power dynamics, advances plot tension.

## Texture Hunting Checklist

When reading historical sources, ask:

- [ ] **Sensory:** What did it smell/sound/taste/feel like?
- [ ] **Material:** What objects did people touch daily?
- [ ] **Spatial:** How did architecture enforce power hierarchies?
- [ ] **Temporal:** How long did actions actually take? (Travel, communication, cooking)
- [ ] **Emotional:** What did people fear, desire, envy?
- [ ] **Social:** How did class/gender/rank manifest in small interactions?
- [ ] **Physical:** What constrained bodies? (Clothing, weapons, disease, weather)

If a source doesn't answer these questions, it's **academic approach — not novelist fuel**.

## Summary: The Paradigm Shift

| Academic Historian          | Historical Novelist              |
| --------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Seeks truth                 | Seeks emotional resonance        |
| Values completeness         | Values selectivity               |
| Stopped by archival silence | Launches from archival silence   |
| Answers "What happened?"    | Answers "What did it feel like?" |
| Research = end product      | Research = invisible foundation  |
| Timeline-focused            | Texture-focused                  |

The novelist's research must be as rigorous as the academic's, but **what gets extracted** is fundamentally different.
