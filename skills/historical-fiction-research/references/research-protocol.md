# Research Protocol: Phase-by-Phase Workflow

## The Core Problem

Most historical fiction fails because writers either:

1. **Under-research:** Write anachronistic dialogue, impossible plots, thin settings
2. **Over-research:** Turn novels into history textbooks, info-dump, "museum prose"

The solution: A phased research protocol that **gathers the right data in the right order** and **knows when to stop**.

## The Three-Phase Protocol

### Phase 1: Macro-Framework (20% of research time)

**Purpose:** Establish the non-negotiable boundaries of historical reality.

**Mindset:** Cartographer, not texture-hunter. You're building the skeleton, not the flesh.

**Time investment:** Brief and focused. Do not get stuck here.

---

#### Step 1: Timeline Anchoring

Identify the **immovable events** that govern your macro-plot.

**What to map:**

- Wars, battles, sieges (dates, locations, outcomes)
- Natural disasters (plagues, earthquakes, famines, floods)
- Political upheavals (coronations, coups, assassinations, elections)
- Major legal/religious changes (new laws, reformations, edicts)
- Technological shifts that impact plot (invention availability, infrastructure changes)

**Sources:**

- Wikipedia (starting point, then verify)
- Academic timelines (Oxford, Cambridge historical databases)
- Specialized chronologies (e.g., _The Oxford Classical Dictionary_ for ancient Rome)

**Output:**

- Simple timeline document or spreadsheet
- Character age/location matrix aligned with historical events
- List of immovable constraints for plotting

**Warning:** Do NOT attempt to move major historical events for plot convenience. This violates the fundamental contract with readers of historical fiction.

---

#### Step 2: Geographical Immersion

Understand the **physical container** of your story.

**What to map:**

- Distances between key locations (how long to travel by horse, ship, foot?)
- Topography (mountains, rivers, coastlines — how do they shape military strategy, trade, escape routes?)
- Architecture (palace layouts, city walls, street configurations)
- Climate (seasonal patterns, weather extremes that impact plot)

**Methods:**

**Best: Walk the ground**

- Visit historical sites if possible
- Photograph sightlines, distances, spatial relationships
- Feel the physical toll of period travel (walk in period-appropriate footwear if safe)

**Examples:**

- C.J. Sansom walked Tudor London sites to understand distances and topography
- Mary Renault traveled to Greece/Crete to study ancient sites

**Good: Virtual cartography**

- Google Maps Street View (for preserved historical sites)
- Historical map archives (e.g., David Rumsey Map Collection)
- Topographical maps (USGS, Ordnance Survey for UK)
- 3D reconstructions (YouTube, academic projects)

**Sources:**

- Historical atlases (_The Penguin Historical Atlas_ series)
- Archaeological site reports
- Period maps (be cautious: often inaccurate, but show period perception)

**Output:**

- Annotated maps with travel times, key locations, sightlines
- Photos or sketches of spatial layouts
- Notes on how geography constrains/enables plot

---

#### Step 3: Political Architecture

Understand the **mechanisms of power** in your period.

**What to map:**

- How does power actually function? (Not just theory, but practice)
- Who has formal authority vs. real influence?
- What are the patron-client networks, factions, alliances?
- What are the stakes of political failure? (Execution, exile, financial ruin, family disgrace)
- What are the mechanisms of corruption, bribery, favor-trading?

**Robert Harris's approach:**
Focus on **timeless, universal political patterns**:

- Ambition and rivalry
- Corruption and bribery
- Gossip and rumor networks
- Threats and leverage
- Public performance vs. private reality

**Critical technique: Peripheral observer POV**

Do NOT view political architecture from the top down (the king's perspective).

View through **peripheral observers**:

- Secretaries (Tiro in Harris's Cicero trilogy)
- Servants (staff overhear everything)
- Minor officials (see mechanics of power without being decision-makers)
- Analysts and ghostwriters (Harris's _The Ghost_)

**Why:** Grounds abstract political structures in lived human experience. Prevents the narrative from becoming too academic.

**Sources:**

- Primary state documents (dispatches, court records, legal transcripts)
- Political biographies (for plot structure only, not texture)
- Social histories focused on court life, patronage, corruption

**Output:**

- Power hierarchy map (who reports to whom, who has leverage over whom)
- Stakes document (what characters risk by losing political struggle)
- Glossary of period-specific political mechanisms

---

**Phase 1 Complete When:**

- You know the immovable timeline
- You understand travel times and geography
- You grasp the power structures and political stakes

**Do NOT:**

- Linger in Phase 1 indefinitely (this is procrastination disguised as research)
- Over-research political minutiae (you're writing a novel, not a dissertation)
- Consult only academic sources (you need texture, which comes in Phase 2)

---

### Phase 2: Texture Hunting (70% of research time)

**Purpose:** Gather sensory data, material culture, psychological paradigms — the fuel for immersive fiction.

**Mindset:** Novelist, not historian. You're hunting **usable texture**, not exhaustive facts.

**Time investment:** This is your core work. Spend the majority of your research time here.

---

#### Step 1: Define Scope (Critical!)

**Rule:** Restrict research to your protagonist's immediate reality.

**Limit by:**

- **Geography:** Specific locations protagonist visits (not entire nation/empire)
- **Social strata:** Protagonist's class and the classes they interact with (not all of society)
- **Timeframe:** Specific years of plot (not entire reign/era)

**Example:**

- **Bad scope:** "Research Tudor England"
- **Good scope:** "Research London merchant-class life, 1532-1536, focusing on legal profession and court interactions"

**Why this matters:** Without scope limitation, research becomes infinite. You'll never draft.

---

#### Step 2: Hunt Sensory Details

**Goal:** Collect visceral, physical texture that makes the world feel lived-in.

**What to hunt:**

**Visual:**

- Colors (dyes, fabrics, building materials)
- Lighting (candlelight, torchlight, natural light — how does it shape perception?)
- Clothing (weight, rustle, restriction, decoration)
- Architecture (how rooms are arranged, what that reveals about power/privacy)

**Auditory:**

- Ambient noise (what did pre-industrial cities sound like? Streets, markets, churches)
- Specific sounds (horse hooves on cobbles, sword against scabbard, loom clacking)
- Speech patterns (accent markers, class-specific vocabulary)

**Olfactory:**

- Smells of daily life (cooking, smoke, animals, human waste, perfumes, incense)
- Period-specific scents (which flowers, which spices, which industrial processes)

**Tactile:**

- Temperature (how did people heat/cool spaces?)
- Textures (rough wool, smooth silk, cold metal, warm wood)
- Physical discomfort (tight clothing, heavy armor, long rides, illness)

**Gustatory:**

- Food preparation methods (roasting, boiling, preserving)
- Taste of period meals (recreate historical recipes if possible)
- Drinking water quality, ale/wine as daily beverages

**Tools:**

- **Odour wheels** (prompt for describing historical aromas)
- **Historical recipes** (cook them to understand taste/texture)
- **Fashion plates and surviving textiles** (museums, online databases)
- **Archaeological reports** (material culture, tools, household objects)

**Sources (prioritize these in Phase 2):**

1. **Primary intimate texts** (diaries, letters, household accounts) — **highest value**
2. **Material culture sources** (museum catalogs, archaeological reports, paintings)
3. **Social history monographs** (focus on daily life, not macro trends)

---

#### Step 3: Hunt Material Culture

**Goal:** Understand the physical objects that shaped daily life.

**What to hunt:**

**Objects people touched daily:**

- Tools of their trade (looms, quills, weapons, cooking implements)
- Currency (how much did things cost? What could you buy with a week's wages?)
- Household items (furniture, utensils, storage)
- Transportation (horses, carriages, ships — what was travel actually like?)

**Clothing and adornment:**

- How clothing restricted or enabled movement
- How long it took to dress (servants required? Hooks, laces, pins?)
- Class markers in clothing (fabrics, colors, styles)
- Jewelry, cosmetics, hairstyles

**Technology and infrastructure:**

- Lighting (candles, oil lamps, torches — expense, duration, quality)
- Communication (how long for a letter to travel? Courier systems?)
- Sanitation (waste disposal, bathing, disease prevention)
- Timekeeping (clocks, sundials, church bells)

**Methods:**

- Visit museums, request to handle replicas or examine originals closely
- Study contemporary paintings (visual record of objects in use)
- Consult archaeological reports (what survives tells you what was used)
- Read inventories and account books (what people owned, what things cost)

**Sources:**

- Museum online databases (V&A, British Museum, Smithsonian)
- Archaeological site reports
- Period inventories and wills (show what people owned)
- Trade manuals and craft guides

---

#### Step 4: Hunt Psychological Texture

**Goal:** Understand the **thought-world** of the period — what people feared, desired, believed.

**What to hunt:**

**Religious/Spiritual framework:**

- What did people fear about the afterlife?
- What rituals shaped daily life?
- What was the role of religious authority?
- What superstitions governed behavior?

**Social anxieties:**

- What brought shame or dishonor?
- What were the consequences of social transgression?
- How did gossip and reputation function?
- What were the unspoken rules of hierarchy and etiquette?

**Power dynamics:**

- How did class/rank manifest in daily interactions?
- What were the patron-client obligations?
- How did people navigate power imbalances?
- What leverage did the powerless have?

**Illness and death:**

- What diseases did people fear most?
- What was life expectancy for different classes?
- How did people prepare for death?
- What medical knowledge/superstition governed health decisions?

**Techniques:**

**Interrogate primary sources for psychology:**

When reading letters, diaries, dispatches — ask:

- **Why did this person write this?** (Motive)
- **Who was the audience?** (What did they want audience to believe?)
- **What did the writer fear/want/hide?** (Subtext)
- **What are the writer's blind spots?** (Class, gender, cultural biases)

**Example: Hilary Mantel's method**

When reading Eustache Chapuys's diplomatic dispatches:

- Recognized he wrote to impress the Emperor
- Knew he pretended to know more than he did
- Understood his network was biased (papist courtiers)
- Saw his language barrier limited understanding
- Used his very unreliability as characterization fuel

**Sources:**

- Primary intimate texts (diaries, letters — **highest value for psychology**)
- Sermons and religious texts (reveal moral framework)
- Legal codes and trial records (show what society punished)
- Medical texts (reveal understanding of body/illness)
- Etiquette manuals (codify unspoken social rules)

---

#### Step 5: Organize Texture for Retrieval

**Goal:** Store texture so you can retrieve it during drafting.

**Methods:**

**Commonplace Book**

- Traditional: Physical notebook with subject headings
- Modern: Digital equivalent (Obsidian, Notion, Evernote)
- Organize by: Character, Location, Sensory Category, Theme

**The "One File" Method (Michael Pollan)**

- Single massive digital document (50+ pages)
- Dump everything: quotes, sensory notes, trial sentences, structural ideas
- Searchable (Ctrl+F during drafting)
- Grows organically throughout research and drafting phases

**Scrivener/Dedicated Software**

- Import PDFs, web pages, images directly into project file
- Split-screen: Research pane + manuscript pane
- Organize by: Characters, Settings, Themes, Timeline
- Tag and cross-reference

**Aeon Timeline**

- Visual timeline syncing fictional plot with historical events
- Ensures characters are in correct location when historical events occur
- Manages complex chronology and subplots

**Categories to maintain:**

1. **Sensory Texture** (smells, sounds, textures, tastes, visual details)
2. **Material Culture** (objects, clothing, tools, costs)
3. **Psychological Notes** (fears, desires, social rules, power dynamics)
4. **Plot Architecture** (timeline, immovable events, political structures)
5. **Language** (period idioms, vocabulary, speech patterns — for dialogue)

---

**Phase 2 Complete When:**

You can answer these questions **viscerally, not academically:**

- What does your protagonist's world smell like?
- What sounds fill their daily environment?
- What objects do they touch most often?
- What do they fear more than anything?
- How does power manifest in the architecture of rooms they enter?
- What does food taste like at their social level?
- What physical discomforts shape their day?

**If you can't answer these sensory questions, keep hunting texture.**

---

### Phase 3: Subconscious Incubation (10% of research time)

**Purpose:** Transfer conscious knowledge to subconscious fluency.

**Mindset:** Trust the process. Let go of conscious control.

**Time investment:** Psychological break between research and drafting.

---

#### The "Forget It All" Technique (Robert Harris)

**Harris's rule:** Gather 10x more information than you'll use, then **actively forget it** when drafting.

**Why?**

The conscious mind cannot:

- Juggle complex historical data
- Maintain narrative flow
- Preserve emotional resonance
- Sustain character voice

**All at the same time.**

**Solution:** Push historical data to the **subconscious** ("the boys in the basement" — Harris's term).

**How it works:**

1. **Intensive immersion** (Phase 1 + 2): Saturate conscious mind with period details
2. **Psychological break** (Phase 3): Stop researching, close research files
3. **Incubation period**: Days or weeks where you don't actively think about research
4. **Drafting**: Trust that texture will surface organically through character action

**What happens:**

- Subconscious retains psychological reality of the period
- Conscious mind freed to focus on narrative flow, pacing, emotion
- Historical details emerge naturally, not forced
- Character voice feels authentic, not academic

---

#### Hilary Mantel's "Cinematic" Drafting

Mantel described her process as **cinematic unfolding**:

**Not:** Wide, objective historical overview
**Instead:** Character's narrowed field of vision, immediate physical reality

**Example from Wolf Hall:**

She doesn't describe the Tudor court from an omniscient historical perspective. She shows what **Cromwell sees** in the moment:

- Cold cobble against his cheekbone (after a beating)
- Smell of beer and blood
- Sound of his father's footsteps
- Narrow, urgent, physical

**The history is there** (accurate court dynamics, political tensions), but it's **felt through the body**, not explained academically.

---

#### Practical Steps for Incubation

**1. Close research files**

- Do not keep Scrivener research pane open during drafting
- Do not consult sources mid-scene to verify facts
- Trust your immersion work

**2. Insert placeholders for missing details**

When you encounter a gap in knowledge during drafting:

**Do NOT:**

- Stop writing to research it
- Break narrative flow

**Do:**

- Insert placeholder: `[CHECK: What did Roman bridles look like?]`
- Make best guess and keep writing
- Note it for later fact-checking

**3. Maintain prose momentum above factual precision**

**Drafting priority:** Emotional truth, narrative flow, character voice
**Revision priority:** Factual accuracy, anachronism auditing, precision

**Separate drafting and revision mindsets.** Do not confuse them.

**4. Take a research sabbatical before drafting**

If possible, insert a deliberate gap:

- Finish Phase 2 research
- Take 1-2 weeks off (read fiction, walk, do something unrelated)
- Begin drafting with fresh subconscious fluency

---

**Phase 3 Complete When:**

- You can draft scenes without consciously consulting research
- Period details surface naturally in character action
- You trust your subconscious to handle historical accuracy
- You're writing from **psychological fluency**, not academic knowledge

## Common Mistakes and Fixes

### Mistake 1: Getting Stuck in Phase 1

**Symptom:** Weeks spent reading academic histories, political biographies, macro analyses
**Problem:** Academic approach — not novelist fuel
**Fix:** Set a hard deadline for Phase 1 (e.g., 2 weeks). Force yourself into Phase 2.

### Mistake 2: Infinite Texture Hunting

**Symptom:** Never feeling "ready" to draft, always finding one more source
**Problem:** Research procrastination (fear of drafting)
**Fix:** Set a research deadline. If you can answer the sensory questions viscerally, you're ready.

### Mistake 3: Skipping Phase 3

**Symptom:** Drafting immediately after research, constantly consulting sources mid-scene
**Problem:** Academic voice intrudes, info-dumping, stilted prose
**Fix:** Take a deliberate break. Close research files during drafting. Trust your subconscious.

### Mistake 4: Under-Researching Constraints

**Symptom:** Plot resolves through mechanisms impossible for period (fast travel, instant communication)
**Problem:** Thin, unmoored world that breaks reader suspension of disbelief
**Fix:** Go back to Phase 1. Verify travel times, communication limits, technological constraints.

## Next Steps After Research

Once research is complete:

1. **Plan the thriller structure** → Use `harris-thriller-planner` skill
   - Choose POV character (Tiro technique)
   - Define ending first (John Irving rule)
   - Map historical events to thriller beats

2. **Draft scenes with texture** → Use `historical-scene-drafting` skill
   - Write "men in rooms" political dialogue
   - Ground in sensory details without slowing pace

3. **Revise for thriller urgency** → Use `thriller-revision` skill
   - Apply modern analog filter
   - Extract exposition, cut bloat

4. **Audit for authenticity** → Use `historical-authenticity-review` skill
   - Final anachronism sweep
   - Dialogue polish

The research is **invisible foundation**. If you've done it correctly, readers will never notice the work — they'll only feel the lived-in reality.
