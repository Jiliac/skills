# Source Evaluation for Historical Fiction

## Evaluating Sources Like a Novelist, Not an Academic

Academic historians ask: "Is this source reliable?"
Historical novelists ask: "What texture can I extract from this source?"

Even deeply biased, unreliable sources can yield rich psychological texture if interrogated correctly.

## Hilary Mantel's Method: Interrogate the Source

When Mantel researched _Wolf Hall_, she didn't just read the diplomatic dispatches of Eustache Chapuys (Imperial ambassador to Henry VIII's court) for facts. She interrogated the **psychology** of the writer.

### Questions to Ask of Any Historical Source

1. **Who wrote this, and why?**
   - What did the writer want?
   - Who was the intended audience?
   - What would the writer gain from lying or exaggerating?

2. **What is the writer's access to power?**
   - Are they in the room where decisions are made?
   - Are they relying on secondhand gossip?
   - What is their position in the social hierarchy?

3. **What are the writer's blind spots?**
   - Language barriers (Chapuys struggled with English)
   - Cultural biases (Catholic viewing Protestant court)
   - Class assumptions (noble viewing peasant actions)
   - Gender limitations (male writer describing women's private spaces)

4. **What is the writer's emotional state?**
   - Fear (writing under threat of punishment)
   - Ambition (trying to impress a superior)
   - Grief (mourning a personal loss)
   - Anger (seeking revenge through written record)

### Example: Chapuys's Dispatches

**Academic use:** Verify dates, locations, attendees at court events

**Mantel's novelistic use:**

- Chapuys wrote to make himself look good to the Emperor
- He pretended to know more than he did
- He relied on a specific network of courtiers with a "papist bent"
- His language barrier meant he misunderstood English court nuances
- His biases revealed what Tudor courtiers wanted him to believe

**Result:** Mantel used Chapuys not as objective truth, but as a deeply flawed character whose very unreliability revealed the psychological texture of the Tudor court's manipulation and propaganda.

## Source Types and Texture Yield

### 1. Primary Intimate Texts (Highest Value)

**Examples:** Diaries, personal letters, household accounts, private journals

**Why valuable:**

- Unfiltered access to "thought-world" of the period
- Reveals daily anxieties, not just major events
- Provides vernacular, speech patterns, idioms
- Shows what people worried about when no one was watching

**Texture yield:**

- Sensory details (what they ate, wore, smelled, heard)
- Psychological patterns (fears, superstitions, prejudices)
- Social dynamics (gossip, rivalries, alliances)
- Material culture (costs, tools, household objects)

**Examples from masters:**

- Robert Harris: Samuel Pepys's diary for English Civil War novels
- C.J. Sansom: Legal correspondence for _Shardlake_ series

**How to find:**

- University digital archives (many diaries digitized)
- Historical society collections
- Published primary source compilations
- Family archives (for local history)

### 2. Material Culture & Archaeology (High Value)

**Examples:** Surviving garments, tools, weapons, furniture, paintings, archaeological reports

**Why valuable:**

- Direct sensory experience of physical objects
- Reveals constraints of period (weight of armor, restriction of corsets)
- Shows what was actually possible (not just what was written about)

**Texture yield:**

- Weight, texture, temperature of objects
- Physical limitations (clothing, tools, weapons)
- Spatial arrangements (how rooms were organized)
- Color, pattern, decoration aesthetics

**Techniques:**

- Visit museums, request to handle replicas
- Recreate historical recipes (taste, smell, texture)
- Use odour wheels to reconstruct historical smells
- Study contemporary paintings for visual details

**Example:** C.J. Sansom walked the topography of historical sites to understand physical distances, sightlines, and the toll of period travel.

### 3. Social History Monographs (Medium-High Value)

**Examples:** Books on daily life, lower-class economics, medicine, agriculture, domestic work

**Why valuable:**

- Fills in what primary sources omit (the lives of the illiterate, the poor, the marginal)
- Provides mechanics of survival (how people earned money, cooked food, avoided disease)
- Reveals period-specific constraints often invisible to elite writers

**Texture yield:**

- Daily rhythms and work patterns
- Economic realities (costs, wages, barter systems)
- Medical knowledge and disease fears
- Food preparation and meal structures

**Warning:** Social history can still be too academic. Prioritize books focused on **lived experience** over demographic statistics.

**Good titles include:**

- "The Time Traveler's Guide to [Period]" series (Ian Mortimer)
- "Daily Life in [Period]" series
- "The [Period] Household" books

### 4. State/Diplomatic Texts (Medium Value)

**Examples:** Ambassadorial dispatches, court records, treaties, legal transcripts

**Why valuable:**

- Essential for plotting major events
- Reveals power dynamics and political maneuvering
- Provides subtext (what is said vs. what is meant)

**Texture yield:**

- Political architecture (how power functions)
- Coded language and euphemisms
- Legal constraints and consequences
- Threat assessment and risk calculation

**Warning:** These sources are **highly biased**. Writers shape events to serve their interests. Must be interrogated for psychology, not accepted as objective truth.

**How to use:**

- Map writer's motives and audience
- Identify what they wanted reader to believe
- Use bias itself as characterization tool
- Cross-reference with other sources to find contradictions

### 5. Academic Biographies & Macro-Histories (Low Value for Texture)

**Examples:** "Great Man" biographies, geopolitical histories, economic analyses

**Why necessary:**

- Timeline alignment (when did immovable events happen?)
- Political context (who had power, who was at war)
- Geographic boundaries (borders, territories, travel routes)

**Why insufficient:**

- Top-down view (kings and generals, not lived experience)
- Abstract analysis (not sensory or emotional)
- Often ignores material culture and daily life

**How to use:**

- Phase 1 only (establish macro-framework)
- Extract timeline, then move to higher-value sources
- Do not rely on these for character voice or sensory details

**Rule:** If you're still reading academic biographies in Phase 2 (texture hunting), you're doing academic research, not novelist research.

## Robert Harris's Preference: "History Unfiltered"

Harris actively avoids secondary historical interpretations. He doesn't want historians' analysis; he wants direct access to period voices.

**Why?**

- Historians impose modern frameworks on past events
- Academic analysis flattens the strangeness and immediacy of period sources
- Interpretation filters out the psychological texture novelists need

**Harris's method:**

- Read the speeches, letters, diaries directly
- Skip the historian's introduction and analysis
- Let the voices of the period speak without academic mediation

**Example:** For his Cicero trilogy, Harris relied on Tiro's (real historical figure) shorthand notes of Cicero's speeches, not modern scholarly interpretations of Roman politics.

## Dorothy Dunnett's Meticulous Material Research

Dunnett (Lymond Chronicles, Niccolò series) was legendary for material culture research.

**Her method:**

- Studied historical recipes to understand taste and food preparation
- Examined period paintings for clothing, posture, room arrangements
- Researched trade routes to understand what goods were available where
- Investigated period crafts (glassblowing, weaving, metalwork) to describe processes accurately

**Result:** Her novels feel materially grounded. When characters handle objects, readers sense weight, texture, and function.

## Mary Renault's Topographical Research

Renault (The King Must Die, The Persian Boy) traveled extensively to ancient sites.

**Why?**

- Understand distances (how long does it take to walk from Athens to Sparta?)
- Grasp topography (how do mountains shape military strategy?)
- Feel spatial dynamics (how does architecture enforce power hierarchies?)

**Result:** Her novels have accurate physical geography. Battles, journeys, and political maneuvering are grounded in real terrain.

## Red Flags: When a Source Won't Help Fiction

**Skip or deprioritize sources that:**

- Focus only on demographic statistics without human stories
- Provide economic theory without material culture
- Analyze power structures without showing how they felt to individuals
- Offer macro trends without micro examples
- Use academic jargon that can't translate to character voice

**Example of unusable source:**
"The fiscal policy of the Tudor crown shifted from demesne-based revenue extraction to parliamentary taxation, reflecting the transition from feudal to early modern state formation."

**Why unusable:** No sensory details, no human experience, no character voice. Pure academic analysis.

## Triangulation: Cross-Reference Multiple Sources

Never rely on a single source, even if it's primary.

**Why?**

- Every source has bias, blind spots, limitations
- Cross-referencing reveals contradictions that create texture
- Multiple perspectives show how different social positions experienced the same event

**Example:**

- Elite courtier's letter: "The banquet was a grand success."
- Servant's account book: Lists massive food costs, suggests financial strain
- Ambassador's dispatch: Notes the king looked unwell, suspects poisoning attempt

**Novelist's use:** The contradiction reveals the gap between public performance and private reality. The banquet was performative spectacle masking financial and political crisis.

## Summary: Evaluating Sources for Fiction

| Source Type                    | Fiction Value   | What to Extract                                               |
| ------------------------------ | --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Primary intimate texts         | **Highest**     | Psychological texture, vernacular, daily anxieties            |
| Material culture & archaeology | **High**        | Sensory details, physical constraints, spatial dynamics       |
| Social history monographs      | **Medium-High** | Daily life mechanics, economic realities, survival strategies |
| State/diplomatic texts         | **Medium**      | Power dynamics, coded language, political architecture        |
| Academic biographies           | **Low**         | Timeline only, then move to higher-value sources              |

**The novelist's job:** Extract texture from every source, even unreliable ones. Interrogate the psychology of the writer. Use bias as characterization fuel.
