# Historical Fiction Research

Research protocol designed specifically for historical novelists. Prioritizes texture (sensory details, material culture, power dynamics) over academic facts, following the Robert Harris methodology.

## When to Use

- Build research plan for new historical novel
- Find usable texture (sensory details, material objects, daily life)
- Evaluate historical sources for fiction utility
- Audit manuscript for anachronisms (factual, tonal, conceptual)
- Balance period accuracy with reader comprehension

## Core Principle: Novelist vs. Academic Research

**Academic historians** seek objective truth, macro trends, exhaustive verification.
**Historical novelists** seek texture, sensory data, psychological paradigms.

If a methodology prioritizes demographic shifts over how cobblestones feel underfoot, it's **academic approach — not novelist fuel.**

## The Research Protocol

### Phase 1: Macro-Framework (~20% time)

**Timeline anchoring** - Immovable events (wars, coronations, disasters)
**Geographical immersion** - Walk ground or use maps/streetview
**Political architecture** - Power mechanisms through peripheral observers

### Phase 2: Texture Hunting (Primary Focus)

Gather sensory data and usable texture. This is the novelist's core work.

**Usable texture** = Historical details that advance story, deepen character, or root reader in physical moment
**Inert facts** = True data that does nothing to propel narrative or illuminate human condition

**What to hunt:**

- Sensory details (smells, textures, sounds, tastes)
- Material culture (clothing weight, tool mechanics, food preparation)
- Power dynamics (how status manifests in architecture, address, seating)
- Daily rhythms (work patterns, travel times, disease fears)
- Psychological paradigms (religious terrors, superstitions, social anxieties)

**How to hunt:**

- Interrogate sources for psychology, not just facts (ask: "Why did this person write this? What did they want?")
- Use odour wheels, historical recipes, fashion plates to reconstruct sensory experience
- Restrict scope to protagonist's immediate geography, social strata, timeframe

**Time investment:** ~70% of total research

### Phase 3: Subconscious Incubation

Robert Harris: Gather 10x more info than you'll use, then **"forget it all"** when drafting.

The conscious mind cannot juggle historical data while maintaining narrative flow. Push acquired data to subconscious. Trust that immersion has altered your baseline understanding.

When drafting, insert placeholders for missing facts. Maintain prose momentum. Fill gaps in revision.

## Source Hierarchy for Fiction

Not all sources yield novelist fuel. Prioritize ruthlessly.

| Rank  | Source Type                                                          | What It Provides                                      | Fiction Value                            |
| ----- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| **1** | Primary intimate texts (diaries, letters, household accounts)        | Unfiltered thought-world, vernacular, daily anxieties | **Highest** - Psychological texture      |
| **2** | Material culture & archaeology (garments, recipes, paintings, tools) | Direct sensory details, physical boundaries           | **High** - Sensory scaffolding           |
| **3** | Social history monographs (daily life, lower classes, medicine)      | Mechanics of survival, what people eat/fear/earn      | **Medium-High** - Contextual scaffolding |
| **4** | State/diplomatic texts (dispatches, court records, treaties)         | Plotting and subtext (but biased, must interrogate)   | **Medium** - Plot architecture           |
| **5** | Academic biographies, macro-histories                                | Timeline alignment only                               | **Low** - Necessary but insufficient     |

**Rule:** If a source only provides macro-political data without grounding it in human experience, it's academic approach — not novelist fuel.

## Anachronism Detection Framework

A single glaring anachronism "pricks the bubble of belief." Audit across three categories:

1. **Factual** - Objects, technology, events in wrong time (easiest to prevent)
2. **Tonal** - Vocabulary, idioms, register clashing with era (requires linguistic research)
3. **Conceptual** - Modern psychology, morality, science imposed on historical characters (hardest to detect, most insidious)

**The "Tiffany Problem"**: Details historically accurate but _feeling_ anachronistic to modern readers. Weigh narrative value — if it breaks immersion without serving vital purpose, substitute or add author's note.

See `references/anachronism-types.md` for the full detection framework with examples, diagnostic techniques, and confidence levels.

## Common Research Pathologies

### Museum Prose

Overly reverent, sterile, artificial narrative voice from treating past as exhibit.

**Symptom:** "Forsooths," hyper-formal syntax, archaic term overload
**Cause:** Valuing academic accuracy over human empathy
**Fix:** Characters didn't view their world as archaic. To them, it was modern, vital, pressing.

### Info-Dumping

Compulsion to prove research was done by including data blocks.

**Symptom:** Massive exposition on Tudor hierarchy, Regency fashion, monastery layouts
**Test:** Does story function if this paragraph is removed? If yes, delete it.
**Fix:** Reveal info through action, dialogue, protagonist's direct interaction with environment

### Under-Researching

Thin, unmoored work from relying entirely on imagination without material grounding.

**Symptom:** Plot resolves through mechanisms impossible for time period (fast travel, instant communication, ignoring class/gender strictures)
**Fix:** Understand specific constraints - travel times, disease lethality, communication limits

## Data Management

How you store texture dictates how easily you retrieve it during drafting.

**Options:**

- **Commonplace book** - Subject-organized notebook for random texture
- **One File method** (Michael Pollan) - Single massive, searchable doc with all readings, sensory notes, trial sentences
- **Scrivener/Aeon Timeline** - Import sources directly, split-screen with manuscript, coordinate fictional plot against real events

**Categories to maintain:**

- Sensory data
- Psychological observations
- Plot architecture (timeline, immovable events)

## Quick Reference Checklist

**Before researching:**

- [ ] Define protagonist's immediate geography, social strata, timeframe
- [ ] Prioritize primary intimate texts and material culture
- [ ] Avoid macro-histories until Phase 1 complete

**During texture hunting:**

- [ ] Interrogate source psychology (motives, biases, access to power)
- [ ] Collect sensory details, not just facts
- [ ] Note usable texture vs. inert facts

**Before drafting:**

- [ ] Achieve psychological break from research
- [ ] Trust subconscious to surface data organically
- [ ] Prepare to insert placeholders for missing details

**During anachronism audit:**

- [ ] Factual check (OED, museum databases)
- [ ] Tonal check (dialogue vs. narration)
- [ ] Conceptual check (mindset audit for modern psychology)
